### Now: better options
**(same outcome, more social, more “Wardrobe”)**


---
1) Option A — “The Pronouns Arrive as a Gift”

**Mechanism:** someone uses “she” casually in a warm moment, and it *lands*.

* Lucy (or Tahlia) tells a story, laughs, says: “She saved my arse on that seam,” meaning Charli.
* No one corrects it. Not because it’s “the rule,” but because it feels… true in the room.
* Charli’s response is the tell: not a lecture, just a tiny, involuntary softness — a breath he didn’t know he was holding.

**Why it works:** it’s not enforced; it’s *offered* — and accepted by silence.

**Beat you can write in:** later, privately, Celeste says something like:

> “If that didn’t feel right, you can tell me.”
> and Charli can’t quite meet her eyes when she says,
> “It felt… nice.”

That’s all you need.


---
2) Option B — “Protective Solidarity” (the girls close ranks)

**Mechanism:** “she” appears when a man is present — because women use language as a boundary.

* A delivery guy, client, or tradie does the classic male thing: calls Charli “mate,” “champ,” “he,” or looks her up and down with that evaluative squint.
* Sarah steps in, bright as a blade:

  > “She asked for it to be left by the back door, thanks.”
* Lucy backs it without hesitation. Tahlia follows. The whole room holds the line — not angry, just *settled*.

**Why it works:** it frames “she” as *women protecting one of their own* — and it makes the pronoun shift a **boundary tool**, not a gender debate.

**Extra benefit:** it foreshadows anti-androgens as part of *boundary maintenance / comfort / alignment*, not “compliance.”


---
3) Option C — “The Wardrobe Language: Roles, Not Essence”

**Mechanism:** pronouns become tied to *function in the room*, not metaphysics.

Wardrobe can have an unspoken linguistic habit:

* Everyone in the fitting cycle (measurements, pins, stays, shifts, petticoats) is referred to as “she” because the workflow is women’s clothing, women’s silhouette logic, women’s body vocabulary.
* It’s not “you are a woman therefore she.”
  It’s “you are in the women’s workstream; you are addressed as women are addressed here.”

**Why it works:** it’s almost *professional dialect*, like a kitchen calling everyone “chef.” No ideology. Just culture.

Mara, crucially, would not romanticise it. She might grunt something like:

> “If you’re on the women’s line, you take women’s language. It keeps the work clean.”
> Which sounds stern — but it’s not moral judgement; it’s *craft clarity*.


---
4) Option D — “Charli Doesn’t Ask — She Stops Correcting”

**Mechanism:** the shift happens because Charli quietly permits it.

This is *very* believable:

* At first, someone says “she,” Charli half-lifts a hand to correct it… then doesn’t.
* Next time, she lets it pass faster.
* Soon, “he” feels like a splinter — not because anyone outlawed it, but because Charli has stopped identifying with the friction it causes.

**Why it works:** it keeps Charli’s agency intact. The girls aren’t enforcing. They’re following Charli’s cues.

Celeste can later say (soft, observant):

> “You stopped flinching.”
> And Charli realises she did.


---
5) Option E — “Document Drift” (the ledger does it quietly)

**Mechanism:** “she/her” becomes real because it appears in writing *after* it’s already socially true.

This one is very Wardrobe, and avoids “pronoun decree” entirely.

* A ledger entry is written by Lauren or Celeste:
  **Wear-tester: Charli. Notes: She flagged underarm stress at reach; she corrected pin tension; she requested extra tape at the waist seam.**
* Charli sees it later. There’s a moment. Not melodrama — just… a pause.

**Why it works:** the ledger doesn’t argue. It *records reality*. And once reality is recorded, it becomes standard *without anyone making a speech*.

If you want the gentlest possible pivot, do this.


---
6) Option F — “The Girls Make ‘She’ a Membership Tag”

**Mechanism:** they treat “she” like the simplest sign that Charli is inside the circle.

You hinted at this — and it’s potent if done delicately.

* Lucy and Tahlia are talking about some women-only annoyance (safety, clothes, being underestimated).
* Charli offers something practical — not mansplaining, just competent support.
* Lucy says, offhand but sincere:

  > “That’s what I mean. She gets it.”

No applause. No announcement. Just inclusion.

**Why it works:** “she” becomes *kinship grammar* — and it makes anti-androgens later feel like Charli choosing comfort inside that kinship.

### A really effective sequence
**(if you want it to feel inevitable, not forced)**

If you want the change to land like a slow sunrise:

1. **Indirect “she”** (third-person when Charli isn’t in the room)
2. **Protective “she”** (used in front of an outsider — boundary moment)
3. **Ledger “she”** (written down; reality formalised quietly)
4. **Direct “she”** (someone says it to Charli’s face, warmly, without ceremony)
5. **Charli’s micro-acceptance** (she doesn’t correct; later she admits it felt like belonging)

By the time anyone could accuse Wardrobe of “enforcing,” the truth is: no one *did* anything. The room simply followed where Charli was already going.

---

### How this improves
**the anti-androgen segue**

If “she/her” is framed as *belonging + reduced friction + comfort*, then anti-androgens can be framed the same way:

* not “I must become”
* but “I want the noise turned down.”

You can treat it like skincare-as-triage, but endocrine: **a practical choice that makes her nervous system quieter**, makes her body feel less like it’s arguing with her day.

And importantly: it remains *her decision*, taken in a world of women who don’t push — they simply *hold the standard of care*.
