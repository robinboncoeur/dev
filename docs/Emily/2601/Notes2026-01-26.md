## Making Sarah see “home Celeste + home Charli” without it feeling contrived 🧵🪡

You’re absolutely right to be wary: “come over for no reason” can read like the author moving chess pieces.

So the trick is: **give Celeste an *adult, practical* reason that only *incidentally* produces the emotional outcome you need.** Then the reader believes it. Sarah being there becomes a byproduct, not a setup.

Here are a few options that preserve your *no secrecy* principle, while also protecting the needle you described (Wardrobe’s reserve ↔ home’s warmth/spice).

---

## Option A — Heatwave alibi: “If you’re going to melt, do it at my place.” ☀️🧊

**Why it’s believable:** extreme heat makes people pragmatic and communal. Nobody wants to sit in a sweltering rental, commute twice, or risk the pub during a bad air day. Celeste can be brisk about it—*competent hospitality*, not gushing sociability.

**Celeste’s stated reason (clean, non-emotional):**

* “School holidays. You’ll be climbing the walls.”
* “It’s going to be obscene tomorrow—come by tonight. We’ve got shade, cold drinks, and you can sanity-check a thing with me.”

**The real reason (subtext):**

* Celeste wants **Sarah as a witness** to what Charli looks like when she’s safe.
* Celeste wants to quietly **collect the social story**: how the girls talk about Charli when Celeste isn’t there, what they’ve noticed, what’s “settled” culturally at Wardrobe.

**What Sarah sees (the payoff):**

* Charli barefoot or in soft socks, hair down, moving like she’s not apologising for existing.
* Celeste casually touching Charli in tiny ways—*ownership without performance*: a hand at the waist as she passes, a kiss in the doorway as if it’s punctuation.
* And crucially: Charli’s eyes. Not “work careful.” Just… *there.*

**Spice (tiny, safe):**

* Sarah makes one dry remark (“Well. Look at you.”) and Celeste doesn’t blush—she *agrees* with the premise.
* Charli laughs—an actual laugh—and then immediately looks to Celeste, as if asking permission to keep laughing. Celeste answers with a look: *Yes. Keep it. It’s yours.*

**How to keep it from feeling like a staged reveal:**

* Make Sarah arrive with something practical (ice, groceries, a cheap desk fan).
* Make there be a small “task” (salad assembly, drink mixing, slicing fruit) so everyone has *hands busy*—it stops the scene from becoming a sit-down interrogation.

## Option B — “Sunday coffee, but make it a boundary lesson.” ☕️🧭

You mentioned pub vs coffee. Coffee is brilliant for *tone control*.

**Why it works:** it’s public enough to feel normal, but intimate enough for Sarah to clock micro-dynamics. Then Celeste can escalate: “Come back for ten. We’ll show you the place.”

**Celeste’s stated reason:**

* “We’re not a secret. Come have coffee. And no—don’t make it weird.”

**What Sarah sees:**

* Celeste navigates the world with Charli like she’s done it a hundred times: ordering, paying, choosing a table, positioning Charli away from foot traffic. Not fussing—just… *leading*.
* Charli is softer, more talkative, and a touch more daring because it’s not Wardrobe.

**The pivot home:**

* They pass by the house on the way back. Celeste turns it into logistics: “You’re here. Come in. Hydrate. Five minutes.”

**The emotional punch:**

* Inside, Sarah sees the domestic shorthand: where Charli’s bag goes, where Celeste’s keys land, what shelf has Charli’s things now (not tucked away—*placed*).

## Option C — The “non-social social”: Celeste asks Sarah to help with something for Charli 🎁🧵

This one is *sneaky-good* because it’s flattering to Sarah and *centres Charli* without turning her into a topic.

**Celeste’s stated reason:**

* “I need your eye. Not Wardrobe eye—real-world eye.”

Examples:

* A simple outfit choice for a non-work outing (swim day? family thing? appointment?).
* A hair decision (“I’m not letting her do anything drastic without someone sensible present.”)
* A small home fitting of a modern garment that echoes Wardrobe skills (hemming jeans, altering a blouse).

**Why it doesn’t feel contrived:** it’s mission-based. Sarah is invited for competence, not intimacy.

**What Sarah sees:** intimacy happens *around* the task:

* Celeste handing Charli a glass of water without asking.
* Charli responding to Celeste’s tone instantly—no defensiveness, just alignment.
* A gentle moment where Celeste corrects Charli’s self-minimising with one sentence that lands like a stamp:
  “Don’t shrink in my house.”

---

## 1) The heat logic: make it feel inevitable, not convenient ☀️🚋

**Potential wobble:** If it’s truly 40°C, people don’t casually drift home and “see how we go.” They start managing risk: water, shade, timing, transit delays, aircon refuge.

**Fix:** Make the heatwave a known factor *before* the coffee ends.

* Phones ping with a heat warning / “trams delayed due to track temperature” / “services reduced.”
* The café is already pulling blinds; the barista says, “It’s going to be brutal after midday.”
* Sarah makes a dry joke that tells the reader she’s not being “lured”; she’s making rational choices:

  > “If I get stuck on a platform today, I’ll simply evaporate.”

This turns the invite into **practical duty-of-care**, not “plot.”

---

## 2) Why Celeste invites Sarah home (without sounding like a setup) 🧭

**Potential wobble:** “Stay for lunch” is fine, but Celeste also wants to be “up to speed.” If that’s too visible, it can feel engineered.

**Fix:** Give Celeste a *clean stated motive* that isn’t emotional.

Pick one:

* “You’re on public transport. I’m not sending you back out in this.”
* “I’ve got leftovers and cold drinks. Don’t be heroic.”
* “We were going to do lunch anyway. Join us.”

Then keep the “I want to know what’s going on” as **subtext**: Celeste listens more than she probes. If she asks anything, it’s light and observant, not interrogatory.

---

## 3) Charli making lunch: keep it warm, not servile 🍞🥗

**Potential wobble:** If Charli “fixes lunch for everyone,” a reader might (wrongly) map it onto gendered service. In *your* world, she’s competent and caring—great—but you don’t want it to read as “the girlfriend serves.”

**Fix:** Make it *shared orbit*, with Charli as the calm centre:

* Celeste sets the table / pours drinks.
* Sarah does one small thing (cuts fruit, tears herbs, opens a packet, carries plates) because she’s not a guest who sits like a lump.
* Charli “hosts” by doing the part she enjoys—assembly, timing, presentation—because it’s soothing and she’s good at it.

And give Charli agency in the moment:

> “Sit. Both of you. I’m making the easy one.”

That single line flips the power: she’s not “helping,” she’s **directing**.

---

## 4) The “stay after lunch” beat: give Celeste a reason to *insist* 🧊🏠

**Potential wobble:** “Celeste insists she stay” can read pushy unless it’s framed as safety + logistics.

**Fix:** Make it about *timing* and *transport*:

* “The worst of it is 1–4. We’ll wait it out.”
* “If you leave now, you’ll bake on the platform.”
* “We’ve got aircon and cold water. You can be sensible at my house.”

This keeps Celeste in her natural mode: decisive, practical, protective—without emotional pleading.

---

## 5) Sarah’s tongue loosens: calibrate why 🍷🗣️

**Potential wobble:** Alcohol as “truth serum” can feel like a shortcut.

**Fix:** Make it **heat + safety + being off-duty** more than the drink.

* It’s one drink, slow, with water beside it.
* Sarah’s looseness comes from *witnessing normalcy* and realising Celeste isn’t going to punish honesty.
* Give Sarah a small moment where she checks Celeste’s face before she says the real thing—then sees it’s safe.

---

## 6) How the information “spills” without becoming a gossip dump 🧵

**Potential wobble:** If Sarah suddenly lists observations (“the girls saw Charli immediately…”) it can become exposition-y.

**Fix:** Let it come out in **triggered fragments**:

* A small domestic sight prompts Sarah to say, “Oh—Lucy said you do that,” and Celeste goes still: “Lucy said what?”
* Or Celeste mentions a detail (“She’s been tired”) and Sarah replies, “No, she’s been *lighter* at work,” which opens the door.
* Or Charli disappears to rinse plates and Sarah uses the gap to say, quietly:

  > “She doesn’t do that voice at work anymore. The apologising one.”

That’s the gold. Specific. Felt. Not a lecture.
