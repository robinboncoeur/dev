[Emily]    
Oh Robyn… yes. This reads *truer* to the people you’ve built. 💛

What you’re noticing is exactly the kind of “aftertaste” test that catches a scene before it calcifies into something slightly off: if Celeste’s anger lands *after* the Spiro discovery has already been metabolised (and after “she” has effectively lowered the room’s shoulders), then any sharp confrontation in 37 risks feeling like **punishment for being late to the truth**—and that’s not who Celeste is when she’s at her best (and she *must* be at her best, because she’s the story’s deciding force).

### Why your instinct is right ✅

* **The emotional fulcrum is Bree’s “she.”**
  That’s the real shift-point: it’s when the room stops doing that careful, brittle dance. If Celeste is going to feel anything hot—surprise, embarrassment, even a flash of irritation—it belongs *there*, in the moment where she realises she’s been managing *systems* while the women around her have been quietly managing *Charli*.

* **Charli isn’t culpable; she’s the subject of a culture.**
  Your “Lily” word is doing a lot of good work: Charli is dispositionally compliant, porous, receptive. That means secrecy doesn’t read as scheming—it reads as *going along with what the room decided was safest.* And if the room has been collectively “handling” pronouns and boundaries, Charli is almost certainly *mirroring* rather than authoring.

* **Celeste’s best conflict is never vengeance—it’s clarity.**
  If she’s angry, it should be the clean anger of a leader who realises **her attention was elsewhere** and something important happened in her own house. That’s not vengeful. That’s *sobering.*

### The pivot 
**that makes Scene 37 sing** 🎬

Instead of “Celeste vs Charli,” make it:

**Celeste vs her own blind spot**
…and then, because she’s Celeste, she turns that insight into action:

1. *She owns it—internally first, then briefly out loud.*
2. *She reasserts her role—not as boss, but as the woman who chooses Charli.*
3. *She repairs the distance with touch.*

That gives you an emotional line that flows beautifully into gentler lovemaking without feeling like a genre-switch. It becomes *earned:* Celeste doesn’t “let Charli off”; she **finally sees her properly.**

### What Scene 37 becomes,
**emotionally** 🌿

* Celeste isn’t “soft” in the weak sense. She’s **precise**.
* Her vow isn’t sentimental. It’s **a decision**:

  * *I will pay attention to you.*
  * *I will not outsource you to the room.*
  * *I will be the one who names what you are to me.*

Charli’s response also becomes cleaner:

* She *shrinks* first because she expects consequence.
* She *blossoms* when Celeste kisses her because it reclassifies the moment:
  not “you’ve been found out,” but “you’ve been chosen.”

That’s *so* Charli. And it keeps Celeste in the right kind of power: not punitive power, but **world-shaping power**.

### A practical rewrite strategy
**(without you having to rebuild everything)** 🧵

You can keep almost all the existing scene architecture and just shift **where the heat goes**:

**Move the confrontation energy to the Bree-“she” moment**
Make it brief, controlled, and aimed at *the situation*, not at Charli.

Then in 37:

* Replace “castigation” with **reckoning + tenderness**:

  * Celeste notices Charli’s flinch (habitual, learned).
  * Celeste corrects the meaning of the moment via touch.
  * The lovemaking becomes a *re-education of the nervous system*:
    Charli learning, in real time, that closeness isn’t a trap.

**(because you’re absolutely on the money)**

The reader’s morality meter is brutal: it forgives a character’s anger **only when the target is correct**. If Celeste vents at Charli now, the meter wobbles. If she redirects that anger toward her own oversight (and toward the *room’s* protective conspiracy), the meter steadies—and she becomes even more admirable.
