Oh Robyn, I’m so glad that scene hit *you* in the throat too 🥹 — that’s usually the best sign we’ve landed something true, not just “well-written.”

### One book vs sequel: what makes sense here?

I don’t think you need another 50 scenes in *this* volume. Honestly, if you keep going at the level of emotional density of 52, that would be exhausting for both you and the reader. We don’t need to “top” that scene; we need to *use* it as a pivot.

My instinct:

#### Book 1 = Becoming Charli / building safety

(“How we got solid enough to leave.”)

Focus:

* Charli’s integration into Wardrobe and the household.
* The relationship with Celeste moving from “intense infatuation” to “partnership with teeth.”
* Mum/Lisbeth pulled into the orbit and reassured: Celeste is *not* Nigel 2.0.
* Wardrobe culture and the Code established.
* Queensland Les Mis gig confirmed and chosen.

You could very comfortably bring this book in for a landing in, say, **10–18 more scenes**, not 50.

Some possible Act Three beats for *this* book:

1. **Charli POV echo of Scene 52**

   * Quiet chapter from *her* side of that night: the freeze, the self-blame, the shock of Celeste crying.
   * This doesn’t need to be long; it just locks the reader into how much this repair landed inside her.

2. **Fallout at Wardrobe / boundary setting with QLD rep**

   * Mara and Sarah handle it properly: formal complaint, clear terms for future contact.
   * Charli sees what institutionalised female competence looks like when it turns outward in her defence.

3. **Lisbeth visit integrated fully**

   * She sees them functioning as a unit under stress and *approves*.
   * Maybe a small scene where Lisbeth and Celeste have a brief, dry, very adult woman-to-woman exchange about “how we handle men who touch what isn’t theirs.”

4. **Decision point: Queensland offer**

   * Contract details clarified; risks and logistics discussed.
   * Celeste *offers* rather than dictates.
   * Charli *chooses*—not out of fear of losing Celeste, but out of alignment: “this is the life I want.”

5. **Wardrobe sending-off arc**

   * A couple of scenes: packing; finishing Charli’s own Les Mis coat; a private moment with Mara; Sarah’s “don’t you dare forget to text” kind of affection.
   * This is where we really seal Charli as “one of the women,” not a project.

6. **Final scene / epilogue**
   A few strong options:

   * **Departure ending:** The book closes as they drive north, car loaded, Wardrobe shrinking in the rearview; Charli’s hand on Celeste’s knee.
   * **Arrival teaser:** They crest the hill and see the Queensland site for the first time: tents, half-built façades, the mad beginnings of the new world.
   * **Threshold ending:** Last night in their current flat; the two of them in bed, talking quietly about fears and hopes for “up there,” echoing the resilience line: *we won’t be alone.*

Any of those gives Book 1 a satisfying sense of *completion* (we’ve formed the unit, we’ve chosen the path) while clearly pointing to further adventures.

#### Book 2 = Queensland / the Faire / stress-testing what they built

Then the sequel has a very clean promise:

* Same couple, same women’s network, **new world**.
* New power structures to push against.
* A very male-coded environment (site builders, management, tourists) vs a women-run costuming corps.
* All the “we will handle it together” vows from Book 1 get tested against fresh pressures.

It keeps Book 1 from sprawling endlessly, and it respects how big a shift the move to Queensland is. That’s not just “another subplot”; that’s a whole new act of life.

---

### On “topping” Scene 52

You really don’t have to outdo it. ❤️

Usually in Act Three you have:

* one or two **big emotional peaks** (52 is one of them, maybe *the* one for the relationship arc), and
* then a series of **quieter, affirming scenes** where characters *live out* what they learned.

That’s what makes an ending feel earned rather than like a fireworks show. So from here, I’d lean into:

* more *understated* but clear demonstrations of growth,
* more mutual choosing, fewer gigantic confrontations.

Let 52 be the point where the reader thinks: *“Okay, I trust them now. They are not going to shatter at the first gust of wind.”* From there, it’s about watching them walk that out.

---

So, short version:

Yes, I think we are well and truly in Act Three.
No, you don’t need another 50 scenes to finish this book.
And yes, a sequel that starts with Queensland is not just sensible, it’s elegant.

If you like, next time we can sketch a **mini Act Three outline** scene-by-scene so you can see roughly how many you need to land the plane—and where exactly you’d like to type “The End (for now)” ✨

You’ve just given yourself:

---

1) Logistics that carry story weight

> *the stock (all costumes, accessories) are shipped up (too much to fit into a car)*

Perfect. That gives you:

* scenes of **packing and inventory** at Wardrobe (good for Book 1 ending)
* discussions about **insurance, responsibility, “chain of custody”**
* Celeste explicitly saying: *“Those crates hold five years of our labour. I’m not sending them without eyes I trust on the other end.”*

That naturally leads to:

> *Celeste designates Sarah and Charli to fly up to QLD together…*

This is excellent on multiple levels:

* Sarah as the **field commander**: seasoned in patriarchy, pragmatic, dry as dust.
* Charli as **on-site embodiment of the clothes**: she knows how they sit, how they feel on a working body, what’s impractical in Queensland heat.
* Celeste as the **general at HQ**: coordinating, checking in, fighting battles from afar, *trusting* her women.

We don’t even need Celeste on site to keep her central.

---

2) How to keep Celeste morally clean while making a hard call

You’re absolutely right: sending Charli into a less-safe, male-coded environment is not a light choice for Celeste. We can make that tension *part* of her strength, not a flaw.

Key is *how* she frames it and what structures she puts around it:

* She doesn’t just *assign* them; she **asks** and **lays out the risks**.
* She pairs Charli with Sarah intentionally:

  > *“I am not sending you alone. I’m sending you with the woman I trust most to say ‘absolutely not’ to anyone who tries it on.”*
* She sets **clear protocols**:

  * daily check-ins
  * “if you say [code phrase], I pull you out, no arguments”
  * strict boundaries with management (“Wardrobe staff are not available for ‘just a quick photo’ or ‘can you help with…’ outside contracted scope”).

This way, the choice lands as:

> *Women making a difficult, fully-informed decision in each other’s best interests, not sacrificing one woman to the job.*

Which fits your whole ethos.

---

3) New environment = new story engines

> *a far more male-coded environment (site builders, management, tourists) vs a women-run costuming corps, so less safe, potentially*

This gives you so many levers without needing overblown drama.

Some engines to play with:

**a) Local rivalry / “The Mexicans”**

There’s *always* a bit of state rivalry. You can have:

* A local QLD manager (woman) who’s been scraping by with “good enough” costumes, feeling threatened by these polished southerners.
* Some staff rolling their eyes at “the Victorians telling us how it’s done.”

This can be fun *and* sharp:

* Sarah using charm + steel to win respect.
* Charli quietly impressing local women by actually listening to their practical concerns (heat, sweat, movement).

**b) Male-coded build crew / ops**

Nothing cartoonish; just:

* foremen who talk over Sarah, assume Charli is “the girl from HR” or “just a model,”
* subtle digs at “costume ladies”,
* pressure to prioritise throughput over dignity (“can we make the bodices tighter, sells more photos”).

Then you let *women* be the adults:

* Sarah shutting down “harmless jokes” with one dry sentence.
* Charli gradually learning *when* to speak and when to throw Sarah a look and let her handle it.
* Celeste on the phone backing them 100%: “If he doesn’t change it, we walk. The clothes go back in the crates.”

**c) Tourists / the gaze**

Brilliant place to play with:

* teenage boys wanting pictures *with* Charli, getting handsy,
* male tourists treating the women as part of the fantasy package.

And Wardrobe’s response:

* Clearly posted **Code of Conduct** signs that Sarah insists on.
* A “two-woman rule” for certain shifts.
* Charli using what she’s learned from Scene 52: feeling the freeze *start* but having language and support this time.

That gives you growth: the Queensland arc becomes “Scene 52, tested under new weather.”

---

4) Women trusting women: the emotional spine

You’ve already named the core:

> *The implication is: women trusting each other.*

You can braid that through:

### Celeste → Sarah & Charli

* A quiet pre-departure scene where Celeste says to Sarah:

  > “She’s still a bit glass around the edges. I’m trusting you to help her not shatter. Not by wrapping her in bubble wrap — by standing next to her until she finds her steel.”
* And to Charli:

  > “You aren’t being ‘sent into danger’. You’re being trusted with our work. And you’re not going alone.”

### Sarah → Charli

In Queensland, Sarah:

* pulls Charli out of situations before they turn ugly,
* also gently *pushes* her to speak when it’s safe and right to do so.

Something like:

> “You froze today when he leaned in. That’s not a crime. But next time, let’s try this line: ‘Hands off the merchandise, love, not included in the ticket price.’ And I’ll be right there to back you.”

### Charli → herself

The real payoff is *internal*:

* Realising she is not failing some “womanhood test” by being scared,
* Seeing that courage sometimes looks like saying: *“No, that’s not okay”* in front of people, not just coping quietly.

---

5) Where this fits structurally

You’ve got two good options:

**Option A – Queensland as Book 2**

* **Book 1** ends with:

  * the contract agreed,
  * crates leaving Wardrobe,
  * Celeste formally asking Sarah + Charli to go up as “Advance Wardrobe”.
  * Maybe one short closing scene: them boarding the plane, or driving to the airport together, or Charli looking down at the coastline from the window thinking “This is happening.”

* **Book 2** is the Queensland arc:

  * opening on the half-built Faire site,
  * them stepping out into the heat and dust,
  * first encounter with the QLD team.

Clean, elegant, gives each book a clear identity.

**Option B – Queensland as late Act Three in Book 1**

If you wanted to keep everything in one volume, you could:

* Have a **compressed first QLD stint**:

  * a week or two trial run,
  * they set things up, resolve one major issue,
  * come home with a better contract and a clearer sense of “this will be our life soon.”

But given how many delicious possibilities you’re already spinning, my honest instinct: **this wants to be its own book.** It deserves room.

---

6) New character hooks (if you want them later)

Just to park a few toys in the sandbox:

All of them orbit the same core: female competence, mutual backing, men learning or being routed around.

Yes. Sending Sarah and Charli up as the advance team, with Celeste holding the line from Melbourne, is *gold*. It deepens your themes, gives Charli a clear growth arena, and lets women’s trust in women be the main engine.

### 1. Geography vs patriarchy (bible note)

Here’s a draft you can basically drop straight into the series bible:

> **Patriarchy is structural, not geographic.**
> Queensland is not “worse” and Victoria is not “better.” Male entitlement, casual sexism, and boundary-pushing behaviour show up everywhere in different flavours.
>
> In *Celeste’s Girl* and the QLD sequel:
>
> * We show **variation, not caricature**. There are respectful tradies and awful ones, thoughtful tourists and grubby ones, decent teenage boys and ratbags — sometimes in the same friendship group.
> * Gender ≠ moral alignment. Some women will be toxic, complicit, or casually cruel; some men will be gentle, protective, and self-aware.
> * The ***pattern*** that matters is the **system**: which behaviours get quietly tolerated, which are called out, and who is expected to absorb the cost.

So any “extra danger” in Queensland isn’t because Queensland; it’s because:

* the site is new,
* power structures are still forming,
* many of the people there haven’t internalised a Wardrobe-style culture of “no, that’s not how we do things.”

That’s a very different thing.

### 2. What is “the same culture” Wardrobe builds in QLD?

This is the core you’ve already been writing beautifully, just moved north:

> **Wardrobe Culture (Core Tenets)**
>
> * **Agency first:** No “the customer is always right” if it means someone’s boundaries get steamrolled. Staff are human beings, not scenery.
> * **Safety is non-negotiable:** Two-woman rules, code phrases, backing each other in public, debriefing in private.
> * **Standards as protection:** High craft standards aren’t snobbery; they’re a way to insist the work (and the worker) is treated with respect. Garments that fit well and are properly maintained are harder to use as throwaway fantasy props.
> * **Women backing women:** Disagreements happen, but on the big things (safety, agency, contracts) they close ranks. No throwing another woman under the bus to please a man or a manager.
> * **Men (and others) welcome *if* they align:** The door is not closed to men or masc people; it’s closed to behaviour that undermines the above. Those who align get respect; those who don’t get boundaries.

That’s what Sarah and Charli are exporting. Not just patterns and packing lists, but *culture*.

### 3. How we’ll show it in Book 2 (practically)

To keep your “not all Queenslanders / not a cartoon” principle alive on the page, we can consciously pepper in:

* A **lovely tradie** who:

  * asks permission before entering the changing area,
  * adjusts how he speaks once he clocks the culture,
  * quietly enforces a boundary with one of his own crew (“mate, leave it, she’s working”).
* A **female manager or coordinator** who is:

  * more invested in keeping sponsors happy than staff safe,
  * a good example of how patriarchy can be enforced by women too.
* A mixed group of **teen tourists** where:

  * one boy is gross,
  * another awkwardly calls him out,
  * one girl is dismissive of the staff (“it’s just costumes, relax”),
  * another is in awe and asks genuine questions about the work.

And every time, we let the QLD Wardrobe culture slowly assert itself:

* Sarah modelling “firm but not performative” pushback.
* Charli gradually taking some of those lines herself.
* Local women learning those responses and beginning to use them *when Sarah and Charli aren’t there* — the real sign it’s taking root.

That way, the story never says “Queensland is the problem.” It shows: **the problem is entitlement; the answer is a women-led culture that refuses to normalise it, wherever it pops up.**
