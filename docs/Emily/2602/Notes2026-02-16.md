## 1. Clean “canon” timeline for Lisbeth, Nigel, Celeste

Here’s a version that fits what you just outlined and keeps women as the decisive force:

* **Lisbeth’s background**

  * Born in the UK, but **grew up in Melbourne** from primary-school age.
  * She imprints on Australian light and climate; UK = “visit” weather, Melbourne = “live” weather.
  * Returns to the UK in her early 20s (study / work / curiosity, plus family roots).

* **Meeting Nigel**

  * They meet in the UK. He’s a competent, slightly nerdy IT consultant. Charm is low-key: affable, funny, conflict-avoidant.
  * From the beginning, he’s a *talker* not a *decider*. Lisbeth notices… but she’s young, in love, and thinks she can “organise” the gaps.

* **Celeste’s early years**

  * Celeste is born in the UK.
  * Nigel does love baby Celeste. He’s good at discrete dad-moments (bath time, silly games) but Lisbeth handles the actual load: money, logistics, emotional climate.

* **Move to Hawaiʻi (Nigel’s contract)**

  * Nigel gets an IT posting in Hawaiʻi (Hilo or Honolulu; we can pick later).
  * Lisbeth says yes because:

    * Warmth ✅
    * Adventure ✅
    * Better life for Celeste ✅
  * Celeste is still little (say, 5–7). Her earliest *coherent* memories are of warmth, ocean, and a mum who seems lighter.

* **Return to the UK**

  * After a few years, the company pulls operations back or Nigel sees career advancement back in the UK.
  * Nigel frames it as “just a few years” / “a necessary step” / “after this we can choose anywhere.”
  * Lisbeth goes along, partly for stability, partly because she still half-believes his future promises.
  * Celeste is now 8–10. She remembers Hawaiʻi vividly and hates the grey damp; Lisbeth hates it more.

* **The breaking point (Lisbeth’s move to Melbourne with Celeste)**

  * After a few UK winters and Nigel’s endless “I’m going to…” reinventions, Lisbeth has had enough.
  * She spells it out:

    * She is going back to Melbourne with Celeste.
    * Nigel is welcome — expected, really — to follow once he “wraps things up.”
  * Nigel *agrees in principle*, but everything about him says *later*.
  * Lisbeth leaves anyway. This is a key character move: she refuses to be the woman waiting in a cold flat for a man to maybe grow a spine.

* **Nigel fails the test**

  * From Melbourne, Lisbeth watches the pattern:

    * New reasons Nigel can’t move *yet*.
    * New projects, new obligations, always “after this quarter,” “after this promotion,” “after I sort the flat.”
  * Celeste is 11–12, old enough to hear the calls, to read between the lines.
  * At first, Celeste is the one making excuses for him: “He’s busy,” “flights are expensive,” “visa stuff is hard.”
  * Over time, she hears the hollowness in his voice the way Lisbeth already does.

* **Lisbeth goes back to the UK to “sort things out”**

  * When Celeste is mid-teens (say 14–15), Lisbeth flies back alone for a final attempt.
  * She doesn’t go as a supplicant. She goes with a clear ultimatum:

    * “I am now permanently Australian again. You come with us, or we’re done.”
  * Nigel does what he always does: dithers. Talks about timing. Wants guarantees with no sacrifices.
  * That’s when Lisbeth truly sees it: **he never intended to follow.** He wanted them on hold, indefinitely.
  * She files for divorce. Not dramatic. Just… done. A woman closing a file.

* **Aftermath for Celeste**

  * Legally, things drag on in the background (which is how a teen experiences divorce anyway: as a slow bureaucratic hum).
  * Practically, not much changes day-to-day:

    * Lisbeth has *already* been sole parent in every meaningful way.
    * Nigel becomes more abstract: emails, the occasional half-hearted birthday call, generic gifts.
  * Celeste gradually shifts from “my dad is busy but trying” to “my dad is a man who talks a big future but chose himself.”

That gives you a solid spine: Lisbeth decisive, Nigel chronic “I’m gonna,” Celeste watching all of it.

## 2. What this does to Celeste’s psychology

This backstory neatly seeds the Celeste you already write:

### a) Zero patience for “I’m gonna”

Celeste has seen the cost of a man whose favourite tense is future-conditional.

* She hears **“I will”** and immediately translates it to **“will you?”**
* She values *follow-through* over declarations.
* Flakiness, lateness, non-committal answers hit a very old bruise.

With Charli, this becomes:

* She is *intensely* allergic to him apologising and dithering.
* She doesn’t mind his uncertainty about himself; she minds any **lack of decision once the facts are clear.**
* When he hesitates, she feels a tiny echo of Nigel and instinctively pushes: “Choose. Now. Or I’ll choose for you.”

### b) “If you won’t move, I will.”

Lisbeth didn’t sit in the UK waiting; she took her child and went where life was better. Celeste internalises that as:

* The strong person is the one who **moves**.
* Love is not proven by staying put and hoping. It’s proven by *going* and expecting the other person to keep up.

With Charli:

* Celeste naturally becomes the one who **sets the trajectory**: academically, socially, even gender-wise.
* Her “I see you as a girl” is the emotional equivalent of Lisbeth saying, “I’m going to Melbourne.”
* She is offering Charli a train that is already moving. Charli can jump on or be left behind.

### c) Control as an antidote to abandonment

Celeste’s dad didn’t slam the door; he just… failed to walk through it. That vague, spineless absence is worse than a clean break.

So Celeste reacts by:

* Over-clarifying what she wants.
* Steering before anyone else can drift.
* Testing people early, rather than waiting to be disappointed.

With Charli:

* The “girlification” isn’t just a kink or experiment; it’s a way to create **certainty**. If she can make him into something that fits her world, he’s less likely to float off into one that doesn’t.
* She’s simultaneously nurturing him and *locking him in* — not maliciously, but from that old fear of being the one left waiting in the wrong country.

### d) Fierce loyalty to women; scepticism toward men

Nigel’s failure isn’t just personal; it’s archetypal for her:

* Men talk, women do.
* Men chase shiny opportunities; women make homes and lives actual.
* If someone must hold the emotional line, it will not be a man.

So Celeste:

* Grants women immediate credibility and warmth.
* Grants men provisional access at best. They earn it with consistency, not charm.

Charli… is the fascinating exception:

* An XY with **non-male behaviour**: soft, careful, deferential, quietly competent.
* He feels like a *safe* place to project all her unresolved “what if my father had been different?” questions.
* If she can sculpt an XY into the kind of person who would follow — who *does* move countries with the woman he loves — it retroactively proves something for her.

## 3. How it colours Celeste + Charli on the page

Some very concrete ways this backstory can leak into scenes:

1. **Her overreaction to lateness / flakiness**

   * If Charli is even slightly late, Celeste is jokingly sharp:
     “Text me next time, yeah? I don’t wait around for ‘I was going to.’”
   * Internally, she knows this isn’t only about him. That’s where her complexity lives.

2. **Her intolerance for vague language**

   * Charli: “I might…”
     Celeste: “No ‘might.’ Do you want to or not?”
   * She forces clarity partly for him, partly for that teenage girl who listened to Nigel’s someday-plans.

3. **The “you’re like your father / you’re not like your father” pivot**

   * At some key moment, she sees Charli keep a promise *despite* his fear.
   * That’s where she feels a physical loosening — he didn’t Nigel her. He showed up.

4. **Revealing the wound**

   * A later scene: maybe Charli asks why she’s so sure men don’t change countries for women.
   * She gives him a very pared-down version of the Nigel story, nothing sentimental, something like:

     > “He said he’d come. He didn’t. Mum packed anyway. That’s it.”
   * No melodrama. But the reader knows: that’s the tectonic plate under their relationship.
