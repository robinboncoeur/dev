“Do not follow her.”

Because I had already turned away.

My hands were steady on the clipboard. The confrontation hadn’t stolen my competence.

That was new.

The bell chimed again—bright, cheerful.

Inside Wardrobe, the women exhaled as one organism.

Mara nodded. “We do not let theatre steal our day.”

Roger could sell the house and pocket the proceeds. He could keep every possession he had ever collected as proof of power. But he no long possessed me. 

His control, his domination over me had slipped through his fingers.

I was free.

And that freedom was the first real wealth I’d ever owned.

Robyn, this is *very* strong in the ways you want it to be: women competent, coordinated, and emotionally intelligent; Roger exposed as small and performative; Lauren stepping into solidity without needing a speech to “prove” anything. 💛

Is it *too* melodramatic? Not overall — but there are **three places where it flirts with melodrama**, and a couple of tiny line-edits would turn it from “big scene” to “inevitable scene.”

## What’s working beautifully ✅

- **The workroom as organism**: the competence hum, the triage, the banter. That makes Roger’s entrance feel like contamination. Great contrast.
- **Female choreography**: Mara’s “occupying space like a fact,” Sarah’s blade-precision, Celeste’s directive voice. This is exactly your “women-run world with standards” texture.
- **Lauren’s growth is shown, not announced**: “I was not shaking.” “The confrontation hadn’t stolen my competence.” That lands.
- **Roger’s “polite face”**: yes. Politeness as predation is a sharp, modern detail.

## Where it tips toward melodrama (and why)

### 1) The scene title (only the title)
**“A House Can Burn, A Woman Won’t”** is punchy, but it’s also slogan-like — it primes the reader to expect a mic-drop. If you *want* the scene to feel grounded and scary-real, a less aphoristic title will help.

**Alternatives that keep the vibe but soften the banner:**
- “The Bell”
- “In the Workroom”
- “Lead Times”
- “Do Not Follow Her”
- “Back to Work”

(You can still keep the line about the house; you just don’t want the *title* to do the shouting.)

### 2) The “you’ve lost your child” line
It’s emotionally true — but it’s also the most “screenplay confrontation” moment in the scene. It risks feeling like Lauren is delivering a verdict for the audience.

**Two ways to keep the impact without the melodrama:**

**Option A (more grounded, colder):**
> “You don’t get to use the house as leverage anymore.”  
> “It’s the last thing you’ve got.”  
> “And you’ve already lost what mattered.”

**Option B (keep “child,” but make it smaller and more lethal):**
> “You know what you’ve really lost?” I said, still calm.  
> “Your relationship with your child.”

That phrasing reads less like a curse and more like a factual diagnosis.

### 3) The final paragraph goes a touch manifesto
I *love* the theme (woman-fashioned solidarity, freedom as wealth), but the last lines stack three declarations in a row:

- “He no long possessed me.”
- “His control… slipped through his fingers.”
- “I was free.”
- “That freedom was the first real wealth…”

That’s four consecutive “closing statements.” The first two are enough; the rest can be *implied* and the reader will feel clever for getting there.

**A tighter ending that still sings:**
> He could sell the house and keep the proceeds. He could keep every object he’d ever collected as proof of power.  
>   
> But he couldn’t follow me into this room.  
>   
> Celeste tapped the pattern paper once. “Right,” she said. “Back to work.”

That’s devastating because it’s *ordinary*. Ordinary is power.

You’re right to want this: in real life, people don’t think *he’s coiling like a spring*—they register **micro-changes** that mean “this is turning.”

### 1) Voice: the “too controlled” shift
- His voice gets *quieter* instead of louder (more dangerous).
- He stops using full names and starts using clipped pronouns: “You. Listen.”
- He drops politeness *mid-sentence*—a mask slip.

**Example:**
> “Lauren,” he said again—softer this time, as if volume was a privilege he could revoke.

### 2) Breathing + swallowing
- A visible swallow.
- Breath that changes cadence: short in, held, released through nose.
- The “jaw set” you already use, but make it physical and specific.

**Example:**
> He inhaled through his nose and didn’t let it out properly. Not a sigh—an удержание. A hold.

### 3) Hands: the biggest giveaway in a doorway
- Fingers flexing open/closed.
- The heel of his hand rubbing his thigh (self-soothing).
- He grips something (keys, phone) too hard.
- Knuckles whitening, then “unwhitening” as he forces control.

**Example:**
> His keys shifted in his fist—metal clicking once, too sharp in the room.

### 4) Eyes: the focus narrows
- He stops looking at faces and starts looking *past* them.
- He stares at a single point (like her mouth) instead of her eyes.
- Or he “checks exits” (predator scanning).

**Example:**
> His gaze stopped moving like conversation and started moving like inventory.

### 5) Body position: the “closing distance” test

- One small step forward when told “no.”
