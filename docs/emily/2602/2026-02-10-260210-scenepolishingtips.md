## Emily

Yep — in Australian English **“on the hook” is heard far more as *liable / responsible* (often legally or financially)** than as “you want my commitment/authorisation.” You’ll see it used exactly that way in Aussie legal/business writing and even ATO comms (“you are on the hook for…”). citeturn0search22turn0search13turn0search4

So if your intended meaning is **“you want me to commit / approve / authorise / take responsibility”**, here are very Australian-sounding swaps (pick by vibe):

### Workplace / decision-making
- **“So you want my sign-off?”** (most natural for authorisation)
- **“You want me to approve it?”**
- **“You want me to put my name to it?”** (implies accountability)
- **“You want me to own this?”** (modern, managerial)
- **“You want me to take responsibility for it?”** (plain, direct)

### Casual / blunt Aussie
- **“So you want me to commit, yeah?”**
- **“You want me to back it?”** (support it publicly)
- **“You want me to cop it if it goes pear-shaped?”** (colloquial + consequence)
- **“You want this on me?”** (short, pointed)

### If the subtext is “you’re trying to pin it on me”
- **“Right — you want to put it on me.”**
- **“You’re trying to make this my problem.”**
- **“So if it blows up, it’s me wearing it.”** (very Aussie cadence)

Here’s what I’d suggest—practical, “gentle-but-not-permissive,” and workable day to day.

## 1) Reframe “deflection” as a skill, then out-skill it
When kids are good at verbal sidestepping, the fix isn’t more talking. It’s **fewer words + clear follow-through**.

**Golden rule:** *Don’t debate behaviour with a 4-year-old.*  
You reflect the feeling, state the boundary, give the next step.

**Script (calm, boring, repeatable):**
- “I hear you. **And** the rule is no hitting.”  
- “You can be mad. **You can’t** throw.”  
- “When your hands are safe, then we keep playing.” (*when/then beats threats*)

This is very consistent with evidence-based parent training approaches that reduce disruptive behaviour by tightening contingencies and reducing reinforcement of the “game.” citeturn0search6turn0search1turn0search10

## 2) Make consequences *immediate, small, and predictable*
Gentle parenting works best when consequences are **short, certain, and unemotional**.

Examples:
- Throws toy → toy goes away **for 2–5 minutes** (timer visible).
- Hits → immediate physical boundary: “I won’t let you hit,” step back / move away / remove child from target.
- Screams for demand → pause request until voice is calmer: “Ask again in a calm voice.”

Avoid big, delayed punishments; they don’t teach much at four, and they invite power struggles.

## 3) Don’t require “admission” before repair
If he’s learned to dodge responsibility, don’t make apologies the gateway. Make **repair** the gateway.

Instead of: “Say sorry / admit you did wrong.”  
Try: “We fix it. **What do we do now?**”

Repair menu (choose one):
- “Bring ice pack.”
- “Fetch a tissue.”
- “Help rebuild the blocks.”
- “Sit next to me while I check they’re okay.”

This keeps accountability behavioural, not performative.

## 4) Use *choices*… but only choices you can live with
Choices are great—unless they become another deflection tool.

**Good choices (both acceptable):**
- “Red cup or blue cup?”
- “Shoes first or hat first?”
- “Walk to the car or hold my hand to the car?”

**Not great:** “Do you want to stop hitting?” (invites “no.”)

## 5) “Connection” first—then “direction” (but swiftly)
With kids who’ve had early medical trauma, **co-regulation** matters. But co-regulation doesn’t mean long cuddly negotiations mid-meltdown.

A quick pattern:
1) Name feeling: “You’re furious.”
2) Boundary: “I won’t let you throw.”
3) Regulate: “Breathe with me / squeeze my hands / stomp 5 times.”
4) Re-try: “Now—show me ‘ask voice’.”

The AAP notes disruptive behaviour can be tied to trauma and other underlying issues; addressing the “why” while teaching skills is often key. citeturn0search3turn0search16

- **Visual cue** (picture card, timer).
- **Catch success fast**: “You stopped when I said stop—good listening.” (specific praise)
