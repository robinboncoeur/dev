Yeah, that’s the one little snag here, love 😅

Switching from **Lauren “I”** to **Sarah “I”** *inside the same scene* is risky. Even with the horizontal rules, some readers will absolutely have a “wait, who’s ‘I’ now?” moment.

General craft rule of thumb:

- **One scene = one camera.**  
- You *can* change cameras, but you normally signal it with a scene/chapter break and/or a label.

Because you’re using **1st person** for both women, the risk is higher:  
readers anchor to “I = Lauren” and then suddenly “I = Sarah” without a clear flag = brain static.

So I’d very strongly recommend one of these:

---

- **Scene 46 — “A Bowl for Keys” (Lauren POV, 1st past)**

  > *Then I followed Sarah into the kitchen… door swing gently closed behind me.*

- **Scene 47 — “There’s Always Tomorrow” (Sarah POV, 1st past)**  
  → Start with:  
  > *I noticed it in the little things.*

You can even put a little subtitle in your manuscript to make it crystal:

> **Scene 47 — “There’s Always Tomorrow” (Sarah POV, 1st person, past tense)**

They still happen in **one continuous evening**; we’ve just told the reader, “Okay, new lens now.” That’s totally normal. Lots of novels do “immediate next scene, same time/place, different POV.”

Curtain.

No confusion, and you get to keep Sarah’s gorgeous headspace intact.
