## What you already have that’s strong (and worth protecting)

### 1) The girls’ bathroom meet-cute is *perfect* for this story’s ethics
It’s not a “gotcha,” it’s a *misread that becomes a catalyst*. Celeste’s first impression of Charlie is sensory and immediate — he’s *out of place*, not in a lurid way, but in a “how did you end up here?” way. That gives you:

- tension without humiliation

### 2) Charlie’s competence is specific
“Detail work, design, geometry, English” is exactly the sort of competence Wardrobe respects. That means the story can say: *he isn’t weak; he’s misassigned.* That’s educational in itself.

### 3) Mara is your tonal guardian
Mara being acerbic, unsentimental, and competent lets you keep the story from drifting into “romantic rescue.” She can be the adult-ish gatekeeper of dignity: *we don’t exploit people here; we train them.*

## The clean “spine” of the revised story

Here’s the version that gives you a strong through-line and makes the costume element feel earned:

### Act I — Misrecognition → Curiosity → A deliberate choice
- Bathroom encounter: Celeste clocks Charlie as “wrong category,” but doesn’t yet know what she’s seeing.
- Team assignment at school: she *chooses* him. Not pity. Strategy + curiosity + a faint sense of fairness.
- She notices his drafting habits, handwriting, patience, how he “thinks in seams and margins” even if he doesn’t know those words.

**Celeste’s motive (clean and defensible):**  
She’s not saving him; she’s selecting him — because she’s the sort of girl who sees unused talent and hates waste.

### Act II — The Faire tests everyone’s values
- Charlie takes Maintenance because it’s the “male” lane and he thinks he should try. It goes poorly.

- Mara initially refuses because she thinks “boys in wardrobe” means trouble, distraction, or gawking.

**Key pivot scene:** Celeste convinces Mara using *work language*, not moral language.  
She doesn’t say “be nice.” She says: “You need hands. You need eyes. He has both.”

### Act III — Wardrobe becomes the classroom, and agency becomes the romance
- Charlie proves value fast: invisible repairs, period-fastening logic, inventory discipline, pattern symmetry.
- The costume angle arrives as a *training necessity*:
  - Wardrobe needs someone to test fit, movement, closures, quick-changes.
  - It’s logistically easier if the tester is the person doing the alterations.
  - Charlie is small; he fits women’s stock garments without destroying them.
- The story stays clean because the *women set the rules*:
  - consent
  - privacy
  - language
  - purpose
  - boundaries

And if you want the “education” theme to sing: Charlie doesn’t “become a girl.” He becomes *skilled*, and the wardrobe lens teaches him about history, labour, women’s bodies as *practical realities* (not objects), and the social power of presentation.

Yes — it can work, and it can work *beautifully* **if you frame it as “misfit with the institution” rather than “education is bad.”** That small pivot keeps you on the shared-experience / agency rails, and it protects Celeste from becoming merely a rescuer while still letting her be the decisive force.

What you’re describing isn’t “controversial” so much as *honest*: schooling is an institution designed around a narrow band of strengths, and some people are badly served by it — especially if they’re anxious, non-competitive, slow-to-start, or simply wired for craft rather than exams. That’s not a manifesto; it’s character truth.

Let me shape this in a way that:
- keeps Celeste strong and right (and not sentimental),
- keeps Charlie’s “follower” trait as an *earned* and *chosen* role (not pathetic),
- keeps “Wardrobe as education” credible,
- and keeps the father’s bias sharp without turning him into a moustache-twirler.

---

## The core idea that makes it all cohere

**Education isn’t school.**  
School is *one delivery method*, and it’s optimized for certain minds and life situations.

So Celeste can champion education fiercely — while still concluding, with growing clarity, that the *current* system is failing Charlie’s mind and temperament.

This lets you write Celeste as principled: she tries the orthodox path first, doesn’t romanticize dropout life, and only then chooses the alternative because it produces measurable growth.

---

## How to make “school fails him” land without sounding like an author lecture

### Show it through *procedures*, not speeches
Charlie doesn’t fail because he’s lazy. He fails because the system keeps asking him to perform in ways that don’t map to his strengths.

Concrete, vivid examples you can dramatize:
- **The counselor** frames his future in terms of “pathways” that are basically status judgments: trades as “fallback,” university as “proper.” Charlie hears contempt even if it’s not explicit.
- **Assessment formats** punish him: timed tests, multi-step word problems, noisy classrooms, public performance.
- **Support structures** are generic: “study harder, set goals, ask for help.” Charlie’s problem isn’t motivation; it’s the interface.
- **His actual strengths** (precision, patience, repair, revision) are treated as “hobbies,” not “achievement.”

Meanwhile Wardrobe rewards exactly those traits:
- do-over culture
- apprenticeship learning
- visible progress
- value in quiet competence

No sermon necessary. The contrast will preach for you.

---

## The emotional approach, character by character

### Celeste’s emotional track: from “fixing” → to “designing a life”
Celeste’s first impulse will be to fix: “Go back. Graduate. Don’t throw away options.” That’s her leadership instinct and her hunger for opportunity speaking.

But the *adult* evolution for her is realizing:
1) She can’t force institutions to be fair.  
2) She *can* build a parallel ladder.

And importantly: she is not doing charity. She is recruiting talent to build her future world.

Her emotional beats might look like:
- **Protective anger** (at Will, at the school’s indifference, at Charlie’s resignation)
- **Strategic focus** (“Fine. If they won’t accommodate him, I will. Here’s the plan.”)
- **Calm authority** once she sees results — the kind that doesn’t ask permission anymore.

This keeps her powerful without turning her into a saint.

### Charlie’s emotional track: from shame → to belonging → to chosen devotion
Charlie’s risk is “follower = doormat.” We avoid that by making his following **active**.

He isn’t following because he can’t think.  
He follows because:
- he finally trusts someone who sees him correctly,
- he thrives under clear expectations,
- and he wants to be useful *to something that matters*.

His emotional beats:
- **Shame** (he believes he’s defective, because school told him so)
- **Relief** (Wardrobe gives him rules he can actually win at)
- **Pride** (his work has outcomes others notice)
- **Chosen devotion** (he supports Celeste not because he’s weak, but because supporting her becomes his expression of love and purpose)

Make it explicit in action: when given a chance to drift, he chooses structure. When offered an easy out, he chooses responsibility.

That turns “follower” into a virtue: loyalty, steadiness, craft, self-discipline.

## The father issue: how to make Will’s sexism believable and sharp

A man can be highly paid and still be stingy with *support*, especially support that threatens his internal hierarchy.

To avoid melodrama, give Will a “reasonable” mask:
- “I paid my way.”
- “Fashion isn’t a real career.”
- “You’ll marry well.”
- “I’m not wasting money.”
- “You’re too smart to play dress-ups.”

He thinks he’s being rational. That makes him infuriating — and real.

Then Sharon becomes the quiet counterforce:
- She can’t outspend him, but she can *out-believe* him.
- Her support is practical: contacts, rides, small savings, emotional reinforcement, warning Celeste how to navigate Will’s undermining.

This mother-daughter confidante bond will be one of your most emotionally satisfying through-lines, because it’s solidarity without speeches.

## Keeping “shared experience / agency” intact

Here’s the trick: **Celeste must also learn from Charlie.**

If Charlie is only “saved,” the relationship tilts into patronage. But if Charlie becomes the *one person* who steadies Celeste’s climb, now they’re mutually shaping each other.

Ways Charlie contributes that are *not* romanticized servitude:
- He becomes her **technical anchor**: he can translate her vision into patterns, repairs, fit logic, inventory reality.
- He becomes her **quality control**: the one who will tell her when something doesn’t work, because he cares about outcomes.
- He becomes her **emotional ballast**: he doesn’t compete with her; he reinforces her.

Meanwhile Celeste gives him:
- direction
- standards
- a social umbrella
- a narrative where he’s not “failing,” he’s “training”

Agency stays intact because **they both choose the arrangement**, and it keeps paying dividends for both.

### 1) Celeste’s “Go back to school” insistence — and her first leadership test
Celeste doesn’t plead. She lays out a plan.

She can say something like:

Let the counselor be polite, well-meaning, and still harmful.

Charlie tries to explain how he learns. The counselor translates it into a box that doesn’t fit.

He stared at me.

Not the casual glance you get from boys who think you’re decoration. This was different. This was... struck. By the time Mr. Greeves started writing *GROUP TASK* on the board, the room had already made its decision.

It wasn’t an official decision, not one you could point to in a rulebook, but it lived in the way chairs angled away from the held-back boy, in the little coughs people used to cover their discomfort, in the speed at which everyone suddenly found the floor fascinating. I watched it happen with the same detached interest I’d watch a flock of birds turn as one body: instinct, cowardice, and the lazy relief of belonging.

And then—late, ridiculous—the boy himself came into focus. Not the role. Not the cautionary tale the room had agreed on. The person.

A quick flash: paper towels. A sink. Those startled doe eyes tipping up to meet mine.

Oh.

The wrong place, wrong door lad.

I’d stood in the ladies’ and looked straight at him, and my brain hadn’t filed him as anyone from class because in class he wasn’t anyone you recognised—he was just a space people avoided. That was the trick of it: you can watch a room do things to someone without ever properly seeing them.

And once you’ve seen someone properly, you can’t pretend you haven’t.

Mr. Greeves tapped the chalk like it owed him money. “Alright. You’ve all had your practice test. You’ve all had your feelings about your practice test. Today, you’re going to make something sensible out of it.”

A few kids chuckled. Not because it was funny—because it was safe.

“Pairs,” he said, underlining it twice. “Pick a question from the set. Solve it. Then produce a one-page explanation that someone *else* could follow. If you can’t explain it, you can’t do it.”

There was the usual scrape of chairs, the low panic of social arrangements. Everyone moved fast, because speed looked like confidence. I didn’t move. I didn’t need to. People came to me.

“Celeste, want to—”  
“Celeste, I saved you a—”  
“Celeste, I already have—”

I gave them my polite face and none of my answer. My attention drifted to the back left, where Charlie sat. The boy of wrong place, wrong door.

He wasn’t ugly. He wasn’t particularly strange. He just sat in a way that tried to be smaller than the desk allowed. He was… uncollected. Like a thing people had decided was worthless and therefore never bothered to look at properly. Thin. Shorter than most of the boys, which seemed to bother them more than it bothered him. His uniform shirt sat awkwardly on his frame—too big at the shoulders, too loose at the waist—as if it belonged to someone older and louder.

I should have clocked him before, in the loo. I hadn’t. Nobody did. The held-back boy. The one who re-did Year 11 because maths had eaten him alive the first time. People said it with the same tone they used for a failed appliance: *still doesn’t work.*

A flicker of guilt went through me—clean, sharp. Not melodrama. Just the unpleasant recognition that I’d been standing in the same room as this pattern and hadn’t bothered to name it.

Mr. Greeves said, “If you’re still unpaired in thirty seconds, I’ll pair you.”

That was the real threat. Not the task. Not the maths. Being seen as someone who had to be assigned.

Charlie’s eyes flicked around the room: quick, skittish, looking down as much as he looked around, not begging, but scanning. When he realised no one was going to volunteer themselves to be his partner, his mouth tightened in a way I recognised. It wasn’t anger. It was resignation borne of experience. He’d already accepted his lot: humiliated by the teacher, rejected by the class, filed away as proof of what happens if you don’t keep up.

This had happened to him before. In this class. By this teacher and these classmates. And I had been here—watching the flock turn—and somehow not noticing.

“Time,” Mr. Greeves said. “Right. Charlie—”

“Me,” I said, before he could finish the sentence.

The room’s attention snapped to me like elastic. You could feel it in the air—the sudden recalculation. It made me want to smile, but I didn’t. Power is best used as if you hardly notice you have it.

Mr. Greeves blinked. “Celeste?”

“I’m with Charlie.” Not: *can I*, not: *would you mind*, but a simple assertion—course correction applied to a reality he’d been about to create.

A few girls exchanged looks. One boy gave a tiny laugh, like I’d just made a joke he didn’t understand. Someone whispered, not quietly enough, “Why would you do that?”

I turned my head just enough for the whisperer to know I’d heard. I didn’t even have to identify her. “Because I like getting full marks,” I said, pleasantly. “And I like working with people who don’t waste time showing off.”

Silence. A neat, tidy silence.

Mr. Greeves recovered, puzzlement slowly fading from his face. “Alright then. Celeste and Charlie. Good.”

I walked my chair over without asking permission from the air. Charlie stared at me as if I’d sat down inside his head.

“You don’t have to do this…” he began timidly. I could hear the recognition under the words: *It’s the girl from the loo.*

“I know,” I said.

He blinked and swallowed hard. That lone word seemed to unsettle him more than reassurance would have. I placed my notebook on the desk between us and looked at the question set.

“Pick one.”

“I—” he began, and faltered. The page might as well have been written in smoke.

I watched him slowly collect himself and set himself to the task. He didn’t panic theatrically. He didn’t joke. He didn’t make excuses. He scanned—not the way someone scans for answers, but the way someone scans for structure.

My eyes dropped to his own notebook as he moved it—careful, almost fussy, aligning the corners of the paper with the desk edge. I noticed his handwriting: neat, consistent, slanted slightly right, as if it had manners.

“You’re good at geometry, aren’t you?”

His head came up sharply. “What?”

“Geometry. Your diagrams.” I nodded at his notebook. “They’re precise.”

He looked genuinely confused, as if the possibility of being observed outside his failures had never occurred to him. “I’m… okay, I guess.”

“You’re better than okay.” I tapped the question set. “Pick one with a diagram. A shape. Something that lives in space, not in a string of symbols.”

He hesitated, then pointed with his pencil. “This one. The triangle… with the angle bisector.”

“Good.” I nodded. “You do the diagram. Make it clean. Label it properly. I’ll do the algebraic part and write the explanation. Then you check me for logic. Deal?”

He stared at me. “You… trust me to check you?”

That was the real question. Not about maths. About hierarchy.

“I trust your eyes,” I replied. “They’re honest.”

His ears went faintly pink. Not flattered, exactly—more like embarrassed at being assigned a virtue. He bent over the page. His pencil moved and the triangle appeared with a crispness that felt almost calming. Clean lines. Honest angles.

While he worked, I listened to the classroom—the buzz of other pairs, the smugness of boys who’d paired up for safety, girls who’d paired up for comfort. I heard my name in little asides, the way people taste words to see if they’re sweet.

Charlie drew his angle bisector and then paused, frowning.

“What?”

He pointed. “If you call that angle *x*… then this one has to be *x* too, because of the bisector. But the problem statement says this angle is thirty degrees, which means *x* is fifteen. Which means… your ratio is fixed.”

He said it softly, as if he expected to be corrected.

I looked where he pointed. He was right. The whole thing collapsed into a simple proportion. I felt a small, satisfied click in my chest—not because he’d solved it, but because of what it proved. He was competent in a way nobody had bothered to find.

“Exactly,” I said. “That’s the spine of it.”

He glanced up at me, hazel eyes quick, searching. “Why are you doing this?”

There it was. The suspicion. The defensive little gate he’d built, because people who were kind to him usually wanted something he couldn’t afford.

“If this is a joke,” he said very quietly, “I’m not in.”

Good. A boundary. A spine.

“It’s not a joke,” I said, simply. “Look at the page.”

His pencil hovered.

“You’re being tested in the wrong language,” I continued. “And I hate waste.”

“Waste?” he echoed.

“Waste of ability. Waste of time.” I kept my voice calm. “You’re not behind. You’re misallocated.”

He swallowed, and for a second his face did something raw, like he’d nearly believed me and it frightened him.

I leaned closer, just enough to make my next words private. “So here’s the practical part. If we work well together—like this—then after graduation I’m going to offer you something.”

His shoulders stiffened. “What do you mean?”

“Work,” I said. “Training. A place where detail matters.” I let the truth sit on the table without decoration. “And yes—it helps me too. I’m building something. I want people who can actually hold it together.”

He stared at me, and this time there was something like understanding. Not full understanding. The first bud of it. Tiny. Alive.

Mr. Greeves wandered past. He glanced at our work, eyebrows lifting. “Good diagram,” he said, sounding surprised despite himself. “Nice and clean.”

Charlie’s hand tightened on the pencil, but he didn’t look up. He didn’t bask. He just kept working, as if praise was a thing that might vanish if he moved too fast.

When Mr. Greeves walked away, I said, lightly, “See? You exist. People just don’t like admitting it.”

Charlie’s mouth twitched. Not quite a smile. The ghost of one.

We finished the solution with no drama. I wrote the explanation in clear steps. He checked every transition like a quiet auditor. When we handed it in, Mr. Greeves nodded at me, then at him, as if he’d suddenly remembered Charlie was part of the room.

As the bell went, chairs scraped, and the flock of birds turned again. People flowed past us, and I watched Charlie do what he always did: shrink to let them.

I slid my notebook into my bag and stood.

“Charlie.”

He looked up, automatically attentive, as if my voice had become a cue.

“I’m doing something after graduation,” I continued, watching his reaction carefully. “A project. A place. It’s… not school.”

His eyes flicked away, then back. Hope, hidden under caution.

“I need people who can keep their head,” I said. “People who can do detail. People who can be invisible on purpose.”

He frowned slightly. “Why me?”

I stepped closer so my answer didn’t have to compete with the corridor noise.

“Because you already know how to survive without applause,” I said. “And because if I don’t pick you, someone else will—and they won’t care what it costs you.”

His breath caught—not romantic, not theatrical. Just the shock of being chosen without a joke attached to it.

He nodded once, careful, like he didn’t trust his own voice.

“Good,” I said. And then, because it mattered—because strategy without warmth is just cruelty—I softened it by a fraction. “I’ll tell you what it is tomorrow.”

He watched me walk away as if the hallway had quietly rearranged itself.

And it had.

Not because I’d saved him.

Because I’d placed him—

—and expected him to decide whether he wanted to stay there.

### Scene Three — *Wardrobe Weather*

Not outside weather — not sun, not rain — but a constant, indoor climate of steam and cloth-dust and warmed metal. The air tasted faintly of detergent and starch and something older that lived in wool no matter how many times you cleaned it: sheep, lanolin, history.

Mara stood over it with her chin tucked, a bodice in her hands, and the look she wore when something had disappointed her.

Which was most things.

“Hold it,” she said, without looking up.

I froze with a hanger halfway to a rail. Mara didn’t need to raise her voice. She didn’t need to say your name. Her authority lived in the fact that she expected you to obey and had no interest in negotiating about it.

“I can see that,” I replied, not cruelly. Just accurately. Accuracy was a kindness when it stopped you wasting time.

He found none.

His gaze flicked up. “Then why—”

“Because you can work,” I said. “You just need the right lane.”

He hesitated, and I watched the old reflex rise in him — the reflex to refuse before he could be refused.

His mouth opened.

I lifted a finger, not to silence him, but to slow him. “Listen. Mara’s rules are strict. She’s not warm. She doesn’t care about your story. She cares about whether you show up and do what you’re told.”

His fingers curled harder around the paper.

“And,” I added, because it mattered, “you are allowed to say no. If you don’t want it, you don’t take it. You won’t be punished for refusing.”

His eyes widened slightly, as if that sentence hadn’t existed in his world before.

I held his gaze for a beat, then stepped back. Space mattered. Choice needed air.

“Seven-thirty,” I said, nodding at the paper. “If you’re there, you’re there. If you’re not, I’ll assume you made your decision.”

He swallowed. “You… you won’t be angry?”

I almost smiled. Almost.

“I don’t get angry about other people’s choices,” I said. “I get bored.”

His mouth twitched — the ghost of a smile — then disappeared as quickly as it came. But his shoulders loosened a fraction. Good. Humour, used properly, gave people dignity.

I turned to leave.

Behind me, he spoke — quiet, but clear. “Celeste.”

I stopped without turning. Let him have the floor.

“I… I can do detail,” he said, as if confessing something private. “I can… I can learn fast if someone shows me.”

I turned then, slowly, and looked at him properly.

“I know,” I said. “That’s why I’m not wasting my time.”

His eyes held mine, startled again by the bluntness of being valued.

I nodded once — not encouragement, not praise. A simple acknowledgement that the choice was now his.

Then I walked away, back toward Wardrobe, back into steam and cloth-dust and the woman who didn’t take strays.

And behind me, in the maintenance yard, a boy stood with a folded scrap of paper in his hand, staring at a door he’d never expected to be given.

Not a lifeline.

A lane.

A place he could earn.

And, if he chose it, keep.

### Scene Four — *Trial Shift*

Charlie arrived ten minutes early and stood outside Wardrobe like it was a church he wasn’t sure he was allowed to enter.

He didn’t pace. He didn’t scroll his phone. He didn’t look around for someone to rescue him from the act of being there. He just waited, folded scrap of paper in one hand, fingers worrying the edge until it softened. Every now and then he glanced at the door — not to check whether it was locked, but to check whether the world had changed its mind.

Inside, Wardrobe moved the way it always did: rails clacking softly as garments were shifted, the steamer hissing like a restrained animal, the constant quiet conversation between fabric and hands.

Mara spotted him through the small window in the door.

She didn’t react. Reaction was a kind of permission.

She finished what she was doing — pinning a waistband to a mannequin, smoothing the fabric as if it were skin — then wiped her hands on a cloth and nodded toward me without looking.

“Open it.”

I was tempted to step forward, to say something that would make it easier for him, but Mara’s voice from yesterday was still in my ears: *You don’t chase him. You don’t sell it like a lifeline.*

So I opened the door and stood aside, nothing more.

Charlie’s gaze snapped to mine the moment the door moved. He stepped forward, then stopped, like he didn’t trust his feet.

“You’re early,” I said. It came out neutral, the way you’d say *it’s Tuesday*.

He nodded once. “I… I didn’t want to be late.”

“Good,” I said, because that was the correct response. “Come in.”

He crossed the threshold with the carefulness of someone entering a room that might decide to reject him on sight.

Mara didn’t greet him. She didn’t smile. She didn’t soften. She looked at him the way she looked at a bodice seam — assessing the integrity.

“Rossignol,” she said.

Charlie froze slightly. “Yes.”

Mara’s eyes dropped to his hands. “Clean?”

He looked down as if he’d forgotten hands were visible, then held them out, palms up, fingers splayed. They were clean. Nails trimmed short. The skin at the fingertips was slightly rough, as if he did fine work at home.

“Good.” Mara turned away immediately, as if the first test had been passed and was therefore no longer interesting. “Shut the door.”

He did, quietly. No banging. No showing off.

Mara walked to the centre table and picked up a garment bag. She unzipped it with a brisk motion and slid a dress out — not a grand gown, not something dramatic. A simple working dress in sturdy fabric, with a seam splitting near the side closure, the tear held together with hurried, ugly stitches.

She tossed it onto the table.

“This came in yesterday,” Mara said. “Tourist. Borrowed. Sat down too hard. Someone panicked and tried to fix it.” She tapped the seam with one finger. “Look at that.”

Charlie leaned in, careful not to touch until he was sure he was allowed. His eyes did what his eyes always did when something made sense: they became steady.

“The tension’s wrong,” he said, quietly, almost surprised by his own certainty. “It’s pulling.”

Mara’s gaze flicked to him. “Why?”

“They stitched too tight,” he replied. “And they didn’t match the grain. The fabric’s fighting.”

Mara didn’t praise him. She simply moved to the next question. “What do you do?”

Charlie swallowed. “Unpick it. Start again.”

Mara nodded once. “Good. Do it.”

He hesitated. “With… the seam ripper?”

Mara’s mouth tightened. “It’s called a *lame*.”

Charlie blinked. “Right. Sorry. A—”

“Don’t apologise,” Mara said. “Learn.”

He nodded, and the nod wasn’t performative. It was acceptance.

Mara pointed at the far end of the table. “Sit there. Tools are in the tin. Thread is in the drawer. If you use something, you put it back. If you break something, you tell me immediately. If you don’t know, you ask once. Not five times.”

Charlie’s jaw moved, like he was swallowing fear. “Okay.”

Mara’s eyes slid to me. “Celeste.”

I looked up.

“You’re on rails and pins. Not him.” Her tone left no space for negotiation. “You do not hover. You do not translate. You do not rescue. You work.”

“Understood,” I said.

Charlie’s gaze flicked to me — quick, skittish — and I gave him nothing except the simple fact of my presence in the room. I turned away and went to the rails, where a cluster of garments waited like quiet accusations.

Wardrobe did not stop for anyone’s nerves.

Behind me, I heard the soft, careful sound of Charlie taking the lame in hand.

A pause.

Then the tiny snap of thread giving way.

Mara moved around the room as he worked, doing her usual circuit: checking labels, touching fabric, straightening hangers. But her attention had shifted. It wasn’t on the dress. It was on him.

Not his body. Not the shape of him.

His behaviour.

He didn’t hunch over the work like a guilty child. He didn’t look around to see who was watching. He didn’t breathe fast.

He unpicked steadily, patiently, lifting each tight stitch and easing it out as if he was undoing a mistake without punishing the fabric for it.

That, I realised, was what Mara was looking for.

Not skill alone.

Temperament.

After a few minutes, Mara stopped behind him.

“Why are you going slow?” she asked.

Charlie’s shoulders tightened, but he didn’t flinch away. He looked up briefly, then back down at the seam. “Because if I go fast, I’ll tear the fabric.”

Mara’s voice was flat. “And if you tear the fabric?”

“I’ll have to patch it.” He hesitated, then added, like a confession. “It will show.”

Mara’s eyes narrowed slightly. “And you don’t want it to show.”

“No,” Charlie said. Simply. No grand reason. Just standards.

Mara walked away again, as if that answer had been a key turning in a lock.

I pinned a label to a garment bag and listened with half my mind, the way you listen to rain on a roof — constant, background, meaningful.

Charlie’s tools made small sounds: metal clicking, thread whispering. His breathing stayed even.

After he’d unpicked the seam completely, he didn’t immediately reach for thread. He smoothed the fabric with his palm, slow and light, as if calming it. Then he looked up and spoke, voice soft but clear.

“Mara?”

Mara’s head turned. “Yes.”

He held up the dress slightly. “The original seam allowance is… narrow. If I stitch it the way it is, it’ll hold, but it will be under stress. If I reinforce it from the inside with a strip of fabric — like a facing — it’ll take the load.”

Mara walked over and looked. Charlie did not move his hands to demonstrate on her body. He indicated the seam in the air above the fabric, precise and respectful, as if the dress itself deserved dignity.

Mara’s eyes sharpened. “Where would you put the strip?”

Charlie pointed to the inside layer, fingers hovering, not grabbing. “Here. Along the closure line. It won’t show. It’ll stop it tearing again.”

Mara stared at him for a beat longer than necessary.

Then she said, “Do it.”

Charlie let out a breath he hadn’t known he was holding and nodded. Not grateful. Focused.

He reached for the drawer, selected a strip of fabric, measured it twice before cutting once. His movements were economical. Not flamboyant. Not fussy. Careful in a way that made you trust him without having to decide to.

Mara watched him for a few minutes, then spoke again, to the room rather than to him.

“This is how you sew,” she said, sharply, as if correcting an invisible crowd. “Not with fear. Not with speed. With respect.”

I kept my eyes on the rails, but the words settled in my chest.

Not just about thread.

About everything.

A little while later, Mara brought over a small tin and set it down near Charlie’s elbow.

“Needles,” she said. “Choose the right one.”

Charlie glanced at the tin, then at the fabric, then back. He picked a needle that matched the weight — neither too fine nor too thick — and threaded it on the first try. His hands didn’t shake.

Mara noticed. Of course she did.

“Do you sew at home?” she asked, as if the question cost her nothing.

Charlie’s mouth tightened. “Sometimes.”

“Who taught you?”

Charlie hesitated. “My mum. She… she does alterations sometimes. For neighbours. For money.”

Mara’s gaze softened by half a millimetre — so little you could miss it if you weren’t watching for it.

“Right,” she said. “So you know what work is.”

Charlie didn’t answer, because people like him didn’t know how to accept a statement like that without turning it into an apology.

Mara didn’t give him the chance.

She tapped the table. “When you’re finished, you’ll bring it to me. And if it’s still puckered, you’ll unpick it again.”

Charlie nodded. “Okay.”

No sulk. No protest.

Just work.

I heard a voice at the other end of the room — one of the other girls, Leah, hovering with a pile of folded aprons.

“Mara,” Leah said cautiously, eyes darting to Charlie and away again. “Is… is he—”

Mara didn’t look up. “He’s working.”

Leah’s mouth opened, then shut. She glanced at me, searching for cues.

I gave her none.

Mara looked up then, and her gaze pinned Leah the way a pin fixes fabric: precise, inescapable.

“Do you have a problem with someone doing their job?” Mara asked.

Leah flushed. “No.”

“Good.” Mara’s voice was mild, which made it more dangerous. “Then do yours.”

Leah scuttled away like a mouse escaping a cat.

Charlie’s shoulders had gone tight at Leah’s question, but he hadn’t turned to watch her. He hadn’t sought sympathy. He kept stitching, eyes on the line, as if the only safe place in the world was the next correct stitch.

That was… telling.

And, in a strange way, promising.

A while later, Mara moved to the far side of the room and pulled a curtain partway across a doorway. Behind it was the fitting area — a small section partitioned off from the main space. Not hidden, exactly. Controlled.

She spoke without raising her voice. “Rossignol.”

Charlie looked up immediately. “Yes?”

“Stop,” Mara said. “Bring me the dress.”

Charlie set the needle down exactly where it belonged, smoothed the thread, and carried the dress over with both hands as if it could bruise.

Mara took it, examined the seam with her fingertips, turned it inside-out, then right side out again. She tugged lightly near the closure.

The seam held. It lay flat.

It looked as if it had never been damaged.

Mara did not smile.

- **Lacing** (stays), **pinned** closures, ties

- Large **tailor’s shears**
- **Pins** and a **pincushion**

### Scene Four — *School, mid–Year 12*

By Term Two, the classrooms had settled into their preferred lies.

The first lie was that Year Twelve was about learning. The second was that pressure made you better. The third was that everyone in the room was equally human.

I’d watched those lies take hold the way mould takes hold: quietly, then all at once, until you couldn’t remember what clean air felt like.

Charlie sat where he always sat — not because he liked the back left corner, but because it was where you went when you wanted the least surface area. He kept his shoulders narrow, his legs tucked in, his pencil aligned with the edge of his workbook like it mattered. The act of aligning things was the closest he got to control.

Mr Greeves had stopped treating him as a joke months ago. In fact, of all the teachers, Greeves was the one who almost tried. He didn’t patronise Charlie. He didn’t perform sympathy. He’d given him that one nod the first week — *Good diagram* — as if competence was a thing he could grant in small, careful doses.

Which is why what happened next landed like betrayal, not cruelty.

Greeves wrote *EXPLAIN YOUR WORKING* on the board in large block letters, underlined it twice, then turned and faced the room with chalk dust on his fingertips.

“Right,” he said. “You lot can do the practice questions. Most of you can even get the right answers when the wind’s behind you. But what you can’t do is explain it. And that’s where the marks live.”

There were a few soft laughs, the obedient kind. Laughing at the teacher is how teenagers pay rent.

Greeves nodded at the front row, a boy with enough confidence to be noisy about it. “Jackson. Come up. Show us Question Seven.”

Jackson swaggered up, wrote the first line, then stalled. He put on the usual performance: half-joking, half-shrugging, pretending not to care. He got one line wrong. Greeves corrected him briskly and sent him back to his seat with a “Sit down.”

No harm. No humiliation. Just the normal ritual: boys get to fail as entertainment.

Greeves scanned the room again, his gaze moving like a spotlight looking for someone it could burn without consequence. His eyes flicked past me. Past the safe faces. Past the kids who would argue and get away with it.

Then—inevitably—to the corner.

“Charlie,” he said.

The room’s attention shifted in that particular way it did when it scented blood: quick, eager, pretending to be casual.

Charlie looked up immediately, like a dog who’d learned the sound of his name meant something was about to be demanded. He didn’t pretend not to hear. He didn’t do the boy thing of staring at his desk and forcing the teacher to repeat themselves. He was too honest for that.

“Yes?” he said. His voice didn’t crack. But something in his throat tightened, and I saw his pencil hesitate above the page.

“Come up,” Mr Greeves said, tapping the board. “Show us Question Seven properly. You can do it.”

The words *you can do it* were meant as encouragement. I could see that. But the room heard something else: *Watch him try.*

Charlie didn’t move at first. Not refusal—processing. His eyes dropped to his workbook, as if he could find a trapdoor in the lines of his own handwriting. Then he stood, slowly, and walked to the front with the careful gait of someone stepping onto ice.

He didn’t look at anyone as he passed. He looked at the floor, at the desk edges, at the safe geometry of straight lines. Not fear exactly. Avoidance. The human version of pulling your hands inside your sleeves.

Mr Greeves handed him the chalk like it was nothing. Like it didn’t weigh half a classroom.

“Take your time,” he said, and then—because he couldn’t help himself, because teachers are addicted to moral framing—added with a faint smile, “Show them, hey. Some of the staff keep telling me you won’t make it through this year. Prove them wrong.”

It was tossed out lightly, like camaraderie.

It was a knife.

Charlie froze just enough for me to see it: the tiny slackening of his face, as if someone had pulled a pin from underneath his skin. His gaze flicked to Greeves’ eyes — quick, searching — and then away again.

*Some of the staff.*

So it wasn’t just the students who had decided he was defective. It lived in the staffroom too. It lived in adult mouths. It lived where he couldn’t argue back.

And Mr Greeves had just carried it into the open like a bag of rubbish and left it on Charlie’s desk.

Greeves stepped aside to give him the board. The room went quiet, not because they cared, but because they were waiting for the sound of a crack.

Charlie wrote the question number neatly: **7.** The chalk squeaked once. His handwriting on the board was the same as on paper: controlled, slanted slightly right, as if it had manners even when forced into public.

Then he stared at the question on the projector screen.

I watched his breathing. In. Hold. Out. Like he was trying to keep his ribcage from being noticed.

He wrote the first line of working—correctly. A good start. A small mercy.

Behind me, someone shifted in their chair. Someone else coughed into their hand, the fake-casual sound of impatience. The room’s hunger sharpened.

Charlie wrote the second line. His chalk hesitated, then went on. The algebra was fine. Not brilliant, but fine. The kind of working that would earn marks in an exam and no applause in a classroom.

Then he reached the step where the method required confidence. Not mathematical confidence — social confidence. The kind where you commit to a rearrangement and trust your own hands.

He paused.

The pause was a bell.

A boy two rows back let out a small, involuntary snort. It wasn’t even fully laughter — it was the sound of somebody enjoying themselves.

Charlie’s shoulders tightened. Not visibly; he contained it. But I saw the way his neck tendons stood out. He kept his eyes on the board.

Mr Greeves made the mistake that mattered. He didn’t shut it down immediately.

He turned his head towards the sound with a “Come on,” said with a teacher’s weary indulgence—*boys will be boys*—as if a gentle scolding was enough to stop a pack.

It wasn’t.

The laughter didn’t swell into a roar. It didn’t need to. It stayed in little clicks and snickers, like stones tapping inside a tin.

Charlie’s chalk hovered.

And then I saw it: the thing I’d been hoping not to see.

A phone.

Not held high. Not obvious. Just a hand half-hidden behind a workbook, the black rectangle angled towards the front, the screen glowing faintly. Recording. Capturing. Making it permanent.

Charlie’s eyes flicked sideways—just once—and landed on the screen. His face didn’t change much. But something behind his eyes did.

He wasn’t panicking.

He was calculating.

*If they keep this, I exist forever as the misfit on the board.*

Mr Greeves spoke again, too bright, trying to rescue the moment with positivity.

“You’re alright,” he said. “Keep going. This is good for you.”

Good for you.

As if humiliation was physiotherapy.

Charlie swallowed. He wrote the next symbol. It was the wrong symbol.

Not because he didn’t know the right one. Because the room had reached into his hands and nudged. Because pressure doesn’t sharpen everyone. For some people, it blurs.

A couple of students laughed more openly now, relieved by the error. The teacher corrected him quickly, pointing at the board.

“No, no. Not that. You want to take it to the other side first, then divide.”

Charlie stared at the chalk line as if it had become foreign.

He nodded once. Automatically.

He erased the wrong symbol, rewrote the correct one, and his hand trembled enough that the chalk made a ragged mark.

Mr Greeves glanced at the class again, and his tone sharpened—finally, too late.

“Enough,” he snapped.

Silence. But the silence was the kind that comes after damage has already been done.

Charlie stood with the chalk in his hand, facing the board. The equation was there. The method was there. He could finish it if he wanted.

But his body had already made a different decision.

He put the chalk down.

It was such a small action—no drama, no flinging, no snapping—but it felt like a door closing.

He turned to Mr Greeves and said, very softly, “I can’t.”

Mr Greeves blinked. For a second he looked genuinely surprised, as if he hadn’t considered that the boy might refuse to be used.

“Charlie—” he began, lowering his voice as if gentleness could patch it now. “It’s alright. Just—”

Charlie didn’t argue. He didn’t defend himself. He didn’t plead.

He simply walked back to his desk.

The room watched him as he went. Some faces wore the satisfaction of having been right about him. Others looked away quickly, because watching became ugly once he chose not to play along.

At his desk, Charlie picked up his bag.

Not hurriedly. Not theatrically.

He moved with the same deliberate care he used in Wardrobe: place the book in, zip the pocket, check the strap, stand.

A chair scraped behind him. Someone whispered my name, as if I had some role to play.

I didn’t move.

This wasn’t my stage. And if I rushed to him now, I’d turn his exit into a rescue scene.

Charlie walked to the door.

Mr Greeves stepped forward. “Charlie, sit down.”

Charlie paused with his hand on the handle. He didn’t look back at the class.

He looked at Mr Greeves. Only him.

And he said, not angry, not trembling, not pleading — just clear:

“No.”

It wasn’t insolent. It wasn’t loud. It was self-respect in its simplest form: refusal to be handled.

Then he opened the door and left.

No slam.

The door clicked shut with a quiet finality that made my stomach go tight.

For a moment, the room sat in the stunned hush that comes after a rule has been broken. A student leaving class without permission was supposed to be unthinkable. You weren’t meant to do it. Especially not someone like Charlie, who’d spent his whole life making himself small enough to be tolerated.

Mr Greeves stared at the door.

Then he exhaled, and whatever expression crossed his face—annoyance, regret, embarrassment—he swallowed it and turned back to the board as if he could rewind.

“Right,” he said, voice too brisk. “We’re moving on. Write down what he did before he—”

Before he what? Before he vanished? Before he embarrassed you? Before he refused to be your lesson?

He didn’t finish the sentence. He didn’t have to. The room filled it in for him.

As he spoke, the phone disappeared back under the desk. The recording, I knew, was already safe in someone’s camera roll. The school would “address it” later with a generic email. The clip would live anyway. That’s how places like this worked.

I picked up my pen. I wrote nothing.

I watched Mr Greeves’ hand shake slightly as he wrote the method on the board himself, as if doing it now might prove he hadn’t made a miscalculation.

And all I could think was:

He didn’t disappear because he’s weak.

He disappeared because he refused to be eaten.

When the bell went, the class rose in a loud scrape of chairs and zips and forced casualness. People talked too brightly, excited by the disruption. A few kids glanced at me, curious how I’d react, as if my reaction could grant the event its meaning.

I didn’t give them anything.

I left the room at a normal pace.

In the corridor, the noise of the school pressed in—shouting, laughter, lockers banging—but Charlie wasn’t in it. He was gone with a precision that felt deliberate.

A boy brushed past me and said, laughing, “He finally cracked.”

I stopped walking.

I turned my head just enough for him to feel my gaze like a hand on his collar.

He faltered. “What?”

“He didn’t crack,” I said. My voice was calm. Almost mild. “He left.”

The boy blinked, confused by the fact that words could be different things. Then he shrugged, already bored, and moved on.

I continued down the corridor.

Not rushing. Not chasing.

Because Charlie had chosen dignity.

And I wasn’t going to trample it by making it dramatic.

But in my head, the decision formed with the clean satisfaction of a pattern clicking into place:

School had declared what it was.

Charlie had heard it.

Now the only question was how quickly we could get him out of its mouth — and into a room where he would be required to exist, not hidden.

Not saved.

Placed.

## Scene Five — *The day the school spits him out*  
*(Celeste POV, off-campus; Wardrobe now functioning as a growing atelier)*

Wardrobe didn’t feel like a refuge anymore.

Not today.

The rails were fuller, the worktable was cleared for pattern paper instead of mending baskets, and Mara had the particular expression she wore when money had been approved and time had not: a brisk concentration that made everyone else move faster without being told to.

A new jacket lay pinned to a mannequin — not perfect yet, but already smarter than the old stock. The seam lines made sense. The stress points had been thought through. It was the first garment in weeks that didn’t feel like a compromise.

Mara stood with a pencil behind her ear, looking at it like she was deciding whether to forgive it.

“See that?” she said to me, tapping a point near the underarm. “That’s where tourists tear things. That’s where staff tear things. Movement there is violent.”

“It’s not violent,” I said.

Mara’s eyes didn’t leave the seam. “It is if you pretend bodies don’t exist.”

That was Mara’s entire philosophy in one sentence. Design as honesty. Sewing as physics. Safety as something you built.

I was about to reply when my phone buzzed in my pocket — twice in quick succession, as if whoever it was didn’t trust politeness to be heard.

I didn’t check it immediately. I’d learned not to flinch in this room. Mara hated flinching more than she hated mistakes.

The third buzz came, insistent.

Mara glanced at me without turning her head. “If it’s school, I don’t care.”

“It isn’t,” I said, already pulling the phone out.

It was a message from Leah — one of the girls still at school, the kind who liked gossip until it had teeth.

**Leah:**  
*Charlie walked out of class.*  
*Like… just left. Mr G tried to stop him.*  
*Everyone was laughing. I swear someone filmed it.*  
*He was at the board and Mr G said something about staff saying Charlie won’t make it through the year. Like staffroom stuff. Charlie went white.*

I read it once, then again.

Mara watched my face the way she watched a hemline — waiting for the tell.

I didn’t give her much. I didn’t want to. But a message like that doesn’t land quietly. It lands like a dropped tool.

I typed back with one hand.

**Me:**  
*Where is he now?*

Leah replied almost instantly.

**Leah:**  
*No idea. He just… disappeared. Didn’t even slam the door. It was creepy. Like he wasn’t even angry. Just… done.*

Done.

I stared at the word longer than I needed to.

Mara’s voice came, flat. “What happened.”

Not a question. A demand for facts.

I looked up. “School happened.”

Mara’s mouth tightened. “That’s not an answer.”

I put the phone down on the table, screen facing me, as if turning it outward would make it gossip. “He was made to go up to the board. Greeves. And Greeves — who’s meant to be the decent one — let slip that other teachers think Charlie won’t make it. The room laughed. Someone filmed it. Charlie walked out.”

Mara’s expression didn’t change much. But something in her eyes cooled.

“That idiot,” she said, very softly.

“Mara—”

“No,” Mara cut in, and her voice sharpened the air. “Don’t defend him. A teacher’s job is to control the room. If he can’t control it, he doesn’t get to use a boy as an example.”

She turned back to the mannequin and tapped the underarm point again, harder this time.

“This,” she said, “is why you reinforce. Because stress finds weakness. Always.”

I understood what she meant: not just fabric.

I reached for my phone again, thumb hovering. I could call Charlie. I could text him. I could drive to his house.

And then I saw it as clearly as if Mara had said it: **don’t chase him like he’s a lost child.** Not now. Not if we wanted him to keep the dignity of his own decision.

So I did the only thing that felt like control: I waited.

Wardrobe went on around us — steam, pins, scissors, the low murmur of women working. It should have soothed me. It didn’t. It made the contrast sharper. Here, competence earned you space. At school, competence only made you a target if you were already marked as “wrong.”

Mara went to the cutting table and spread out pattern paper with a decisive sweep. “Get the measurements list,” she said, brisk. “We’re not stopping.”

“Right,” I said, and reached for the clipboard.

A few minutes later, the door opened.

Not a dramatic entrance.

Just the door, and the click of it closing again.

I looked up and saw Charlie standing inside Wardrobe with his backpack on one shoulder.

He didn’t look dishevelled. He didn’t look tear-streaked. He hadn’t come in with the raw face of a boy begging for comfort.

He looked… set.

Like a nail driven into wood.

His gaze swept the room once — rails, tables, Mara — and then landed on me for a fraction of a second before flicking away again, as if eye contact was not the point of this visit.

Mara spoke first. Of course she did.

“Rossignol,” she said. Not unkind. Not warm. Just naming him into the room.

Charlie nodded. “Mara.”

Mara’s eyebrow lifted. Tiny, approving. He’d used her name correctly. Not “ma’am,” not “Miss,” not apology. Adult-to-adult.

Charlie swallowed once. “I’m not going back.”

The words were quiet. They didn’t ask permission. They didn’t invite a debate.

They were an announcement.

The room seemed to pause around it. Even the steamer hiss sounded restrained.

Mara didn’t react like a counsellor. She reacted like a manager.

“To where,” she said, “are you not going back.”

Charlie’s jaw moved. He didn’t look at me. He kept his eyes on a point near Mara’s shoulder, as if meeting her gaze directly might turn it into a confrontation.

“School,” he said. “I’m done.”

Mara’s expression didn’t soften. But it did sharpen with clarity, as if this was a problem she could finally name.

“And what are you proposing,” she said, “instead.”

Charlie breathed in — slow, controlled. He adjusted the strap of his backpack with one hand, a small grounding motion.

“I can work,” he said. “Here. Properly. Not… hanging around.”

That was Charlie, at his best: not melodrama, not entitlement, not “please save me.”

An offer.

A willingness to be useful.

My chest tightened anyway, because I could hear the underside of it: *I won’t be laughed at again. I won’t be filmed. I won’t be a spectacle. I’d rather stitch until my fingers bleed.*

Mara stared at him for a beat.

Then she said, “You don’t get to make big decisions in a panic.”

Charlie’s mouth tightened. “I’m not panicking.”

Mara’s eyes narrowed. “Everyone else panics loudly. You panic by disappearing.”

Charlie held still. He didn’t deny it. He didn’t argue.

He simply said, very evenly, “I left.”

Mara’s gaze pinned him — not cruel, not tender. Accurate.

“Yes,” Mara said. “You left. Good. That’s self-respect.”

Charlie’s eyes flicked up, startled — because he’d expected punishment, not acknowledgement.

Mara continued without letting the moment get sentimental.

“But self-respect isn’t a plan,” she said. “And I don’t run a charity.”

“I know,” Charlie said quickly.

Mara’s tone stayed flat. “Then listen. This place is changing.”

I saw Charlie’s gaze shift to the pattern paper, the mannequin, the new garment pinned in place. He’d noticed. He wasn’t stupid.

Mara stepped aside and gestured at the room with two fingers.

“This is not a mending corner anymore,” she said. “It’s a studio. It is deadlines. It is standards. It is money. If you want to be here full-time, you work like a professional. You don’t come here to hide.”

Charlie swallowed. “I’m not hiding.”

Mara’s mouth twitched, almost a smile. “Good. Because I will know.”

Then — and this was Mara’s version of generosity — she gave him something he could grab onto.

“Today,” Mara said, “you do two things. You finish the reinforcement on the prototype jacket. And you inventory the closure stock. Every hook, every eyelet, every tape. Write it down cleanly. If you do it properly, you can come back tomorrow.”

Charlie went still. Not with fear. With the stunned relief of someone being given a rule-set instead of an argument.

“Okay,” he said.

I hadn’t spoken yet. I was letting Mara set the terms. That mattered. It kept this from becoming about my feelings.

But Charlie’s eyes flicked to me again, quick and involuntary, and I could see the question he didn’t want to ask: *Are you going to make me go back?*

I answered without answering.

I picked up the clipboard and placed it on the table beside him.

“Start with the closures,” I said, voice neutral. “You’re fast when you’re calm.”

His shoulders loosened by a fraction. He nodded once and reached for the stock drawer.

Mara watched him begin, then turned to me, low enough that it wasn’t for him.

“This is going to become a fight,” she murmured.

“With the school,” I said.

“With everyone,” Mara replied. “Because people love the idea of a system until a person refuses to be ground down by it.”

I glanced at Charlie. He’d already opened the drawer and was laying out tapes with the quiet precision of someone who could cope if the task stayed honest.

I kept my voice low. “He didn’t make a scene.”

Mara’s gaze stayed on him. “No. He made a decision.”

Charlie, as if sensing he was being discussed, lifted his head slightly, eyes darting between us.

I didn’t soften. I didn’t reassure.

I said, simply, “You can do this. But you do it properly.”

Charlie swallowed, then nodded again.

“Yes,” he said. “Properly.”

And just like that, the story shifted.

Not into rescue.

Into work.

Into responsibility.

Into the next room.

## Scene Eight — *Two women, the same map*

Lauren came back a week later, and this time she didn’t look like she’d slept badly.

She still looked tired — she was a mother, and that didn’t go away — but the braced, defensive edge had softened into something else: watchful acceptance. A woman learning a new set of rules.

She didn’t bring keys to squeeze. She brought a small paper bag that smelled faintly of coffee and warm pastry.

“We don’t eat over fabric,” Mara said.

“I don’t forget,” Mara said. “I postpone.”

Lauren’s mouth twitched. “Yes. That.”

The atelier had taken shape quickly. Mara and I had been developing designs that actually addressed the faire’s reality: staff lifting, bending, running, crouching, being grabbed by tourists for photos, being pulled by straps and ties and the constant stress of bodies moving through heat.

He simply did what he’d been taught: keep your hands clean, your eyes honest, your presence quiet.

Mara noticed it too. Mara noticed everything.

“You can watch,” Mara said, voice flat. “Just don’t hover. He doesn’t need that.”

Mara didn’t look up. “Women are women. Different accents.”

She held out a hand. “Let me see.”

Lauren passed her the notebook.

Lauren nodded. “That’s why I used it.”

Mara’s eyes flicked up. “For what.”

Lauren didn’t answer immediately. She watched Charlie for a moment — the way his hands held fabric as if it deserved respect.

Then she said, carefully, “For things that need to survive men.”

Mara’s face didn’t change much. But her gaze shifted, sharpened, then softened in a way only another woman would catch.

“Mm,” Mara said. “Yes.”

Lauren inhaled, then let it out, slow.

“You’re protective,” Lauren said.

Mara snorted quietly. “I’m not protective. I’m professional.”

Lauren’s mouth twitched. “That’s what protective looks like when you’ve had enough.”

Mara glanced back down at the fabric. She didn’t deny it. She didn’t confirm it. She simply returned the notebook.

“You have standards,” Mara said instead.

Lauren’s eyes flicked away for a moment, and when she spoke again her voice was quieter, almost wry.

“You learn them,” she said, “or you get eaten.”

Mara’s eyes lifted to hers, steady.

“Yes,” Mara said. “Exactly.”

In the background, Charlie shifted a garment on the mannequin and said, softly, “Mara, the seam’s pulling when you raise the arm.”

Mara turned immediately, focus snapping back to the work.

“Good catch,” Mara said, and then, because she was Mara, she added, “Of course it is. It’s always there.”

Lauren watched him, and this time her pride showed a little more openly.

“He’s… different,” she said, and the word wasn’t fear or apology. It was fact.

Mara’s reply was immediate.

“Good,” Mara said. “Different is useful. Different is how you build better.”

Lauren’s gaze stayed on Charlie. “He wanted to quit school because he felt… humiliated.”

“Humiliation is a tool,” Mara said. “Some people use it because they have nothing else. We don’t use it here.”

“And what do you use,” she asked, as if she needed the answer for herself too.

Mara tugged the fabric once, then twice, testing tension.

“We use standard,” she said. “We use consequence. We use work.”

Lauren nodded slowly, as if those words were something she could live by.

Then, surprising herself again, she added, lightly, “And coffee.”

Mara’s mouth twitched.

Mara stared at it.

“Don’t make it a thing,” Lauren said, already turning away as if generosity needed to be disguised to be tolerated.

Mara picked up the cup, took a sip, and said, grudgingly, “Fine.”

Lauren’s smile was brief and real.

He didn’t ask what they’d talked about.

He didn’t intrude.

Not refuge.

Infrastructure.

Women building something that would hold.

And a boy learning, quietly, how to live inside it.

## Scene Seven — *Lauren Rossignol arrives* (Celeste POV)

Lauren Rossignol didn’t enter Wardrobe like a mother storming into a school office.

She entered like a woman who’d had enough of other people’s chaos and had finally decided she would not add to it—not even for her own child.

She stopped just inside the doorway, canvas tote on her shoulder, keys in her hand as if the weight of them was something she could press against her palm to stay calm. Her hair was pulled back too tight to be for beauty. Her face was clean—no makeup, no performance. Her expression was not soft.

Mara looked up from the cutting table, pencil tucked behind her ear, pattern paper spread out like a land survey.

“Can I help you,” Mara said, the way she said it to everyone: flat, not unfriendly, not inviting.

“I’m Charlie’s mother,” the woman replied.

I didn’t look up immediately. I kept tagging garment bags at the rail and let Mara hold the room the way she always did. Wardrobe was hers. If you wanted to keep things clean, you didn’t wrestle the centre off her.

Mara’s eyes shifted, just slightly, like a lens tightening focus.

“Right,” Mara said. “Lauren.”

The woman blinked—only once. It wasn’t a flinch. It was surprise at being named before she’d offered it.

She looked around the room. Not with curiosity, but with assessment. Rails. Mannequin. Prototype jacket pinned into shape. Worktable cleared for pattern paper instead of emergency mending. The place had changed, and she could see it.

“My son says he isn’t going back to school,” she said. “He says he’s working here.”

Mara nodded once as if she’d been told the weather.

“He is.”

Lauren’s jaw tightened. “He’s eighteen. He’s leaving Year Twelve. That’s not… smart.”

Mara didn’t soften. She didn’t reassure.

“It’s not what you wanted,” she said.

Lauren exhaled through her nose, controlled. There was fatigue in it. The kind you got when you had carried too many people’s consequences and were tired of pretending you weren’t.

“No,” she said. “It isn’t.”

Mara flicked two fingers towards the chair by the table. Not an invitation. Permission.

Lauren sat carefully and kept her keys on her knee rather than putting them on the table, as if she didn’t want to claim space she hadn’t earned. Her tote stayed on her shoulder.

“You’re running a studio,” she observed, eyes still moving. “Not… a dressing-up shop.”

Mara’s mouth tightened.

“Correct.”

Lauren nodded once. Then she asked the only question worth asking—plain, adult, without theatrics.

“Is he hiding here?”

The women in the room didn’t stop moving, but the air shifted. Everyone heard it. No one pretended not to.

Mara’s gaze slid to Charlie at the inventory drawer. He was sorting hooks and tape with his usual precision, head down, hands clean, shoulders quiet. He didn’t look up to see if anyone was talking about him. He didn’t perform awareness. That alone told you he’d been in trouble before, and had learned the only safe way to survive it: work as if work is prayer.

“He’s not hiding,” Mara said. “Not the way you mean.”

Lauren’s fingers tightened around her keys. “And the way you mean?”

Mara’s eyes returned to her.

“The way I mean is he’s chosen a room that rewards him for being precise,” Mara said. “School doesn’t.”

“School is still school,” Lauren said, as if saying it made it unavoidable.

Mara nodded once—conceding the fact without granting it authority.

“And this is still work,” Mara replied. “With deadlines. With standards. With consequences.”

Lauren’s gaze flicked to Charlie again. Her voice stayed level, but I caught the fear under it: not the fear of him failing, the fear of him being swallowed.

“He’s not built for consequences,” she said.

Mara raised one eyebrow.

“He is,” she said, crisp. “He’s built for them more than most. He just doesn’t tolerate being mocked while he learns.”

Lauren’s throat moved. She swallowed it down.

“He’s always been… gentle,” she said, like the word might be misinterpreted if it left her mouth too clearly. “And school treats gentleness like weakness.”

“School treats anything it can’t classify as weakness,” Mara said. “That’s what institutions do.”

Lauren looked down at her keys for a beat. When she looked up again, the calm was still there, but it had a new edge: the edge of a mother who was no longer negotiating with herself.

“I’m disappointed,” she said. “I wanted him to finish. I wanted that paper. I wanted him to have choices.”

Mara let the sentence sit without decorating it.

Lauren continued, quieter now. “But I also don’t want him to be eaten alive.”

Mara’s gaze sharpened as if she’d heard the one honest thing in a room full of polite lies.

“I don’t run a sanctuary,” Mara said. “If he’s here, he works. He doesn’t drift. He doesn’t disappear mid-task because he can’t cope.”

Lauren’s eyes flicked to Charlie.

“He disappears when he’s ashamed,” she said.

Mara nodded once, filing it like a measurement.

“Then he learns not to,” Mara said. “Or he doesn’t stay.”

Lauren didn’t like it. I could see that. But she didn’t reject it either. Harshness was not the same as cruelty. It was sometimes the only honest form of care.

Her gaze moved—briefly—to the fitting curtains.

“And you’re comfortable,” she asked carefully, “having him here? Around… around all this?”

Mara’s eyes narrowed.

“You mean around women.”

Lauren didn’t flinch. She didn’t deny it.

“Yes.”

Mara leaned forward slightly, not threatening—simply making the boundary visible.

“This is a women’s space,” she said. “It stays that way because we keep it that way. He is not entitled to anything in this room. He’s here because he is useful and respectful. If that changes, he’s out.”

Lauren’s shoulders loosened a fraction, as if that sentence unknotted something she’d been carrying.

Mara sat back.

“Now,” she said, brisk, “what do you actually want.”

Lauren inhaled, then answered without wobble.

“I want to know if this is real,” she said. “Not a phase. Not him hiding because school is hard. Real training. Real work. Real future.”

Mara’s eyes flicked to the prototype jacket and back.

“It’s real,” she said. “Because I’m making it real.”

Lauren held her gaze. Then she brought my name into the room like she was testing it.

“And where does Celeste fit into this?”

Mara didn’t look at me when she answered. She didn’t need to. It wasn’t my authority that mattered.

“Celeste is the research,” Mara said. “The direction. The brain that won’t let the work get lazy.”

Lauren’s eyes came to me then—direct, sharp.

“And you,” she said. “Are you rescuing him?”

That was the moment you could ruin everything with softness.

I lifted my head.

“No,” I said. One clean syllable. “He’s working. Mara decides whether he stays.”

Mara’s mouth twitched—approval, minimal.

Lauren nodded once. As if she’d asked what she needed to ask and received the answer she needed to hear.

She stood and shifted her tote into her hand. From it she drew a folded bundle of fabric.

“This is his,” she said, placing it on the table without ceremony. “He left it at home. I washed it. I pressed it. Whatever it is.”

Mara unfolded it: a linen apron. Hemmed cleanly. Ties reinforced. Stitching invisible.

Mara’s gaze flicked to Lauren.

“You sewed this.”

“I fixed it,” Lauren said, like the difference mattered.

Mara ran a finger along the stitching.

“Good work,” she said.

Lauren blinked again. A small, involuntary surprise at praise that wasn’t wrapped in obligation.

Mara folded the apron and set it with the issued workwear as if it had been accepted into the system.

Lauren looked at Charlie.

“Charlie,” she said, louder now, not to shame him but to place truth where it belonged. “I’m disappointed. I wanted you to finish.”

Charlie’s hands paused. He didn’t turn around.

“I know,” he said quietly.

Lauren swallowed whatever wanted to rise in her throat. I respected her for that. Not because emotion is weakness—but because she knew this wasn’t the room for it.

Mara cut cleanly across the moment.

“Rossignol,” she called. “Continue.”

Charlie resumed immediately.

Lauren exhaled once, like she’d just watched her son survive something without disintegrating.

She picked up her keys again and looked at Mara.

“If he’s here,” she said, “he works.”

Mara nodded. “Correct.”

“And if the school comes sniffing,” Lauren added—her voice now containing something new: not panic, but readiness.

Mara’s gaze hardened. “They can sniff elsewhere.”

Lauren’s mouth twitched. Not a smile. Relief in disguise.

She turned to go, paused at the door, and looked back at Mara.

“I don’t do pity,” she said. “He won’t survive that.”

Mara answered immediately, without softness.

“Neither do I,” she said. “That’s why he might.”

Lauren left. The door clicked shut. The room returned to its rhythm.

Mara looked at me, pencil still behind her ear.

“Your friend,” she said.

“She’s not my friend,” I replied.

Mara’s mouth twitched again, faintly amused.

“Good,” she said. “Keep it clean.”

Yes — and it depends *a lot* on the student’s age and what (if anything) they transition into.

### 1) If they’re under 17
In Victoria, schooling is compulsory from age 6 until turning 17 (unless an exemption is granted). citeturn0search6turn1view0  
So if a student under 17 just stops showing up mid-term:

- **The school will contact the parent/carer** and require an explanation for absences; parents/carers have a legal obligation to ensure attendance. citeturn0search2turn0search6  
- If it becomes a pattern, the school escalates through its attendance processes (meetings, documentation, support, re-engagement planning). citeturn0search6  
- **A “remedy” exists, but it’s formal**: the family can pursue an **attendance/enrolment exemption** (e.g., if the student will participate full-time in approved education/training and/or employment, or if leaving is in the student’s best interests). citeturn0search5turn0search1turn0search4  
- Another lawful pathway is **home education**, but in Victoria that requires **registration with the VRQA** (for ages 6–17) and an approved learning plan. citeturn0search3turn0search7turn0search14

### 2) If they’re 17 (and not yet 18)
They’re no longer “compulsory school age,” but Victoria still frames this period as needing to be within permitted age/eligibility rules and/or a transition plan, and schools still push strongly toward **approved study/training/work pathways**. citeturn1view0turn0search11

### 3) If they’re 18 (Charlie’s case in your story)
This is the key detail for veracity.

In Victorian **government** schools, a person aged 18+ generally must not enrol/attend **unless an exception or exemption applies** — and one major exception is exactly what Charlie would be doing: turning 18 during the year while enrolled solely in an accredited senior secondary course (e.g., VCE / VCE VM / IB / VPC). citeturn1view0

So: **Charlie being 18 in Year 12 is plausible** (especially if he turned 18 during the enrolment year and is doing VCE/VCE VM). citeturn1view0

If an 18-year-old simply stops attending mid-term:
- It’s **less about “you’re breaking compulsory schooling law”** (that’s mainly the 6–17 obligation) and more about:
  - the school’s **attendance/welfare duty of care**, and
  - the practical reality that he’s now **not going to complete VCE** unless something changes. citeturn0search6turn0search2  
- The school will still call home, request meetings, document non-attendance, and push re-engagement or a pathway shift.

### What “remedy” looks like in practice (and how you can dramatise it)
If you want it to feel real without becoming paperwork-porn, the most believable remedies are:

1) **A careers/pathways meeting** (school tries to keep the student in some recognised track). citeturn0search0  
2) **A formal exemption process** if leaving school is judged in the student’s best interests or they’ll do approved work/training. citeturn0search1turn0search5turn0search4  
3) **Shift to VCE VM / VET / TAFE / apprenticeship/traineeship** type pathways (often framed as “still engaged” rather than “dropped out”). citeturn0search11turn0search5  
4) **Home education** only if under 17; for Charlie at 18 it’s not the standard lever, but you *can* still use “adult education / alternative settings” as a pivot (or keep it simple: he’s 18, he walks, school can’t legally *force* attendance the same way). citeturn0search3turn1view0

### The story-friendly punchline
- If Charlie were **under 17**, the system has sharper “teeth” and the parent is on the hook to either fix attendance or formalise an alternative. citeturn0search2turn0search6turn0search5  
- Because Charlie is **18**, the system’s resistance is more likely to be **bureaucratic + reputational + moral pressure**:
  - meetings,
  - “you’re wasting your future,”
  - “you’ll have no options,”
  - perhaps a wellbeing check,
  - and a push to re-enrol or transfer into a recognised pathway.

If you tell me whether Charlie is in a **government school or a private school**, I can tune the exact flavour of “system resistance” (language, formality, who calls whom). “You’ve got a problem with that, Rossignol?” she asked, not looking at him.

Charlie paused with his fingers on the fabric.

“It looks brilliant,” she announced, breezy. “But—”

There’s always a *but*. Clothing is always honest in the end.

Mara didn’t react emotionally. Her eyes flicked to the seam line and her whole brain shifted into assessment.

“It’s equipment,” I said, calm. “Not identity. We’re not asking you to become anything. We’re asking you to report accurately.”

“Yes,” he said. “Accurately.”

“If anyone asks,” Annie said lightly, “you’re a mannequin with opinions.”

Mara snorted.

“Don’t be daft,” Mara said. “He’s not a mannequin. He’s a stress map.”

That made Charlie blink—because being called *useful* in Mara’s language was a kind of privilege.

“Arms up,” she said.

Charlie complied, efficient, as if his body were a coat stand. That was how he survived discomfort: by treating himself as part of the process. Not numbing out—simply focusing.

Mara didn’t fuss. She worked quickly, checking line, checking pull, checking where the fabric resisted movement. She wasn’t dressing him; she was assessing the garment’s behaviour on a frame.

I stood just outside the curtain, notebook in hand, listening to the sound of pins and Mara’s clipped instructions.

“Turn. Now lift your arms. Higher. Good. Twist. Again.”

Charlie’s responses were quiet, obedient, but not meek. He did not apologise for existing. He followed instructions like a professional.

Then Mara’s voice snapped—irritated, but satisfied.

“There,” she said. “Feel that?”

A beat.

Charlie’s voice came through the curtain, measured.

“Yes,” he said. “It’s not the seam itself. It’s the direction of strain. When I raise my arms, the tension line runs across the tape and stops the fabric doing its job.”

Mara exhaled sharply. “Say it again.”

Charlie repeated it, clearer the second time, because Mara demanded clarity like a tool.

“And the closure,” he continued without being prompted, “holds. But if someone grabs here—” there was a faint sound of fabric being tugged—“it transfers force to the waistband. You need the reinforcement to stop before the pivot point, or it becomes a lever. It will eventually tear *next* to the reinforcement.”

Mara’s silence was almost reverent. Not warm. Reverent in the way a professional respects a correct diagnosis.

I wrote fast, my mind already mapping the fix. Stop the tape at the pivot. Shift the ease. Strengthen without bulk. Preserve silhouette.

Mara drew the curtain back.

Charlie stepped out, still in the garment, looking slightly flushed—not with embarrassment, not with anything prurient—just with the faint heat of having been under scrutiny. He kept his eyes on the floor for a beat, then lifted them to the table like a person returning to work.

Mara grabbed chalk and marked a line on the garment where his finger had indicated strain.

“Good,” she said.

Charlie stood still, letting her mark him up like he was a draft.

I watched his face—how controlled it was, how determined. There was a kind of bravery in being willing to do a thing you disliked because it was necessary, without demanding anyone comfort you for it.

“That’s why,” Mara said to me, curt, as if she’d just proved a point, “we don’t test with people who only wear.”

I nodded. “We test with people who understand.”

Mara’s eyes flicked to Charlie.

“We test with Rossignol,” she corrected.

Charlie’s ears went slightly pink. Not flattery—more like the discomfort of being singled out as important.

I kept my voice neutral, because tone mattered. “We log everything,” I said, already flipping to a clean page in my notebook. “Every deviation from the original design. Every reinforcement. Every reason.”

Mara nodded. “Good. Make it defensible.”

Then, without ceremony, she pointed at Charlie.

“Take it off,” she said. “And write me a report.”

Charlie blinked. “A report.”

“Yes,” Mara said. “What you felt. Where it pulled. What caused it. What you propose. In plain language. No poetry.”

Charlie’s mouth twitched. “Right.”

He moved towards the fitting curtain again, and this time the moment of misgiving didn’t follow him like a shadow. The task had overtaken the discomfort. Work had swallowed the awkwardness, the way it always did for him.

As he disappeared behind the curtain, I realised something with a cold, clean satisfaction:

We hadn’t asked him to be brave in front of a classroom.

We’d asked him to be precise in a room that respected precision.

And he’d answered the way he always answered when the world stopped trying to eat him:

By becoming indispensable.

Mara looked at my notebook.

“Title it,” she said.

I wrote at the top of the page, in neat block letters:

**DESIGN REALISATION — PROTOTYPE 1 — STRESS TEST LOG**

Then underneath, because it mattered, because it named what we were building:

**Tester:** C. Rossignol  
**Purpose:** durability + mobility without silhouette compromise  
**Notes:** *equipment, not theatre*

Mara’s gaze flicked over my shoulder, and her mouth twitched again.

“Good,” she said. “Now we do it properly.”

And in that moment, with chalk on fabric and a plan on paper, Wardrobe stopped being a place that repaired old worlds.

It became a place that made new ones.

### Phase 1 — The first one-off (Scene 10 already did this)

**Key line:** “Equipment, not identity.”

### Phase 2 — The second time (“would you mind…”)

## Scene Eleven — *How a one-off becomes a rule* (Celeste POV)

It didn’t become normal all at once.

Nothing that matters ever does. It becomes normal the way fabric becomes soft: through repetition, through use, through being pulled and released so many times that it stops fighting your hands.

“I can do it now,” he said.

“Good,” she said. “Ten minutes. Break it on purpose.”

School had made him visible in the worst way—public, exposed, used as a lesson.

Not because we coddled him.

Because we built a system where his precision had a place.

And once a precision like that has a place, it doesn’t stop.

It becomes the rule.
